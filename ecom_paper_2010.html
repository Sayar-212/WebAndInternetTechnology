✓ Answer: (b)

SET (Secure Electronic Transaction) was specifically designed by Visa and MasterCard to secure credit and debit card transactions over the internet. It authenticates all parties — cardholder, merchant, and payment gateway — via digital certificates and keeps card details hidden from the merchant.

Q1(ii)A/an ________ is a network that links selected resources of a company's intranet with its customers, suppliers or other business partners.1M
a) Internet
b) Extranet
c) VPN
d) Wide area network
✓ Answer: (b) — Extranet

An Extranet extends a company's private intranet outward to selected external parties (customers, suppliers, partners) via the internet, giving them controlled access to specific internal resources. It is not fully public (internet) nor fully private (intranet).

Q1(iii)How many types of trade cycle are there in e-commerce?1M
a) One
b) Two
c) Three
d) Four
✓ Answer: (c) — Three

The three trade cycle types: (1) Repeat/Commodity cycle — routine repeat purchases (regular supplies); (2) Credit/Large order cycle — large negotiated B2B purchases on credit; (3) Cash/Low-value cycle — small one-off purchases paid immediately (online retail). Each has different EDI transaction sets and processes.

Q1(iv)eBay is an example of a/an ________ e-commerce site.1M
a) B2C
b) EDI
c) C2C
d) B2B
✓ Answer: (c) — C2C

eBay is primarily a C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer) e-commerce platform — it provides an online marketplace where individual consumers buy and sell goods to each other, typically through auctions. eBay itself is the platform/broker (Brokerage model) but the transactions are C2C in nature.

Q1(v)The main purpose of digital signature is1M
a) Non-repudiation
b) Authenticity
c) Confidentiality
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (d) — All of these

A digital signature provides: Non-repudiation — sender cannot deny having signed the message; Authenticity — confirms the message came from the claimed sender; Integrity — detects if the message was altered (not strictly "confidentiality" but the question groups them). The primary ones are authentication and non-repudiation; confidentiality is handled by encryption, not the signature itself. However, in the context of this question, (d) is the intended answer.

c) Communication with other VAN's

✓ Answer: (d) — All of these

A VAN (Value Added Network) is a third-party EDI network that provides: Secure connections (encrypted, authenticated), Store-and-forward (restore transmission) — stores messages until recipient is ready, and Interconnection with other VANs — allowing trading partners on different VANs to exchange messages via VAN interconnect agreements.

A call centre requires cables (network cabling infrastructure), headsets (for agents to communicate hands-free), and LAN (Local Area Network to connect all workstations, servers, ACD, and PBX). Additionally: ACD, IVR, CTI server, agent PCs, UPS — all connected via the LAN infrastructure.

b) Bridges used in an intranet
c) Expensive modem
d) User's application programs
✓ Answer: (a)

Firewalls are most canonically implemented in the router/gateway at the intranet-to-internet boundary — the choke point where all traffic passes. This placement gives maximum visibility and control. While software firewalls exist on endpoints, the primary architectural implementation is at the border router.

Q1(ix)DES is a1M
a) Secret key symmetric algorithm
b) Secret key asymmetric algorithm
c) Public key symmetric algorithm
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (a)

DES (Data Encryption Standard) is a symmetric block cipher — the same single secret key is used for both encryption and decryption. It uses a 56-bit key and encrypts data in 64-bit blocks using 16 rounds of a Feistel network. Symmetric = same key both sides. Today superseded by AES due to the 56-bit key being too short.

Q1(x)Example(s) of e-money is/are1M
a) Credit card
b) Debit card
c) Charge card
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (d) — All of these

All three — credit cards, debit cards, and charge cards — are forms of e-money (electronic money) in the broad sense: they facilitate digital/electronic payment without physical cash. Credit cards provide a credit line; debit cards debit a bank account directly; charge cards require full monthly repayment. All represent electronic payment instruments.

Q2Explain the features needed for a B2B platform.5 Marks
✓ Answer

A B2B (Business-to-Business) e-commerce platform must support complex business needs that B2C platforms do not have:

  • Account-based pricing and catalogues: Different customers (businesses) see different prices, discounts, and product catalogues based on their negotiated contracts.
  • Bulk ordering: Support for large order quantities, case packs, pallet quantities — with quantity-based pricing breaks.
  • Multi-user accounts: A buying organisation has multiple users (purchasers, approvers, finance) — the platform must handle role-based access and approval workflows.
  • Purchase order (PO) support: Buyers raise formal POs — platform must accept PO numbers, create quotes, and generate invoices against POs.
  • Credit terms and invoicing: B2B typically involves 30/60/90-day payment terms — not immediate payment like B2C.
  • ERP/procurement integration: Must integrate with buyers' and sellers' ERP/SAP/Oracle systems via EDI, API, or punchout catalogues.
  • RFQ (Request for Quotation): Buyers request quotes for large or custom orders — platform manages quote-to-order workflow.
  • Reorder management: Automatic or one-click reorder of previously purchased items — business buyers have recurring needs.
  • Compliance and audit trail: Every transaction must be logged for financial auditing and regulatory compliance.
  • Security: Strong authentication, encrypted data, access control — sensitive business data at stake.
Q3Explain SET protocol.5 Marks
✓ Answer

SET (Secure Electronic Transaction) is a security protocol jointly developed by Visa, MasterCard, and major technology companies (including IBM, Microsoft, Netscape) to secure credit/debit card payments over open networks like the internet.

Key Parties in SET

  • Cardholder: Consumer paying with card — holds a digital wallet and certificate.
  • Merchant: Online seller — has a merchant server with SET software and certificate.
  • Payment Gateway: Bank's gateway that processes the card transaction.
  • Issuer bank: Cardholder's bank (issued the card).
  • Acquirer bank: Merchant's bank.
  • CA (Certificate Authority): Issues digital certificates to all parties — root of trust.

SET Protocol Flow

1. Customer browses and selects items → initiates checkout.
2. Merchant sends payment initiation message + certificate.
3. Customer's SET wallet verifies merchant certificate.
4. Customer's wallet creates two messages:
   a) Order Info (OI) — for merchant (what was ordered)
   b) Payment Info (PI) — for bank only (card details)
   → Both signed + encrypted (dual signature — merchant
     sees OI but NOT PI; bank sees PI but NOT OI)
5. Merchant receives OI, forwards PI + authorisation request
   to Payment Gateway.
6. Payment Gateway decrypts PI, forwards to Card Network
   (Visa/MC) → Issuer bank authorises.
7. Authorisation response returned to merchant → order confirmed.
8. Merchant captures payment at settlement.

Security Features

  • Dual signature: Merchant sees what was ordered but NOT the card number; bank sees card number but NOT the order details — privacy for both.
  • Digital certificates: All parties authenticated — no impersonation.
  • Encryption: RSA for key exchange; DES for data encryption.
  • Non-repudiation: All transactions digitally signed.

Note: SET was technically sophisticated but complex to deploy — largely replaced by SSL/TLS + 3D Secure (Verified by Visa / MasterCard SecureCode) in practice.

Q5What do you mean by knowledge engineering and business process redesign?5 Marks
✓ Answer

See 2008-09 paper Q2 for the full detailed answer. Summary:

Knowledge Engineering: The discipline of capturing expert knowledge and encoding it into computer systems (expert systems, AI). Involves knowledge acquisition, representation (IF-THEN rules, frames), validation, and inference engine design. Enables computers to reason like domain experts.

Business Process Redesign (BPR): Fundamental, radical rethinking of business processes to achieve dramatic improvements in cost, quality, and speed. Unlike incremental improvement (kaizen), BPR questions the entire process from scratch. Key steps: identify processes → map AS-IS → identify waste → design TO-BE → implement with IT. Famous example: Ford's Accounts Payable redesign (500 staff → 125 staff, 75% reduction). In ERP context: BPR must happen before ERP implementation.

Q6What is ERP's role in SCM?5 Marks
✓ Answer

ERP plays a foundational role in Supply Chain Management (SCM) by providing the integrated data backbone that connects all supply chain activities:

  • Demand management: ERP captures sales orders and feeds them into production planning and procurement — ensuring supply matches demand. MRP (Material Requirements Planning) module calculates what materials to order and when.
  • Procurement automation: ERP's Materials Management (MM) module automates purchase requisitions, PO generation, goods receipt, and invoice verification — the procure-to-pay cycle.
  • Inventory management: ERP tracks stock levels at every warehouse and plant in real time — enabling optimal replenishment and preventing stockouts or excess inventory.
  • Production planning (PP): ERP schedules manufacturing based on orders, material availability, and capacity — coordinates upstream supply with downstream demand.
  • Warehouse management (WM): ERP manages inbound receiving, storage locations, pick-pack-ship — optimises warehouse throughput.
  • Supplier integration: ERP sends EDI POs to suppliers and receives ASNs and invoices — automating the supplier-facing part of the supply chain.
  • Reporting and analytics: ERP provides supply chain KPIs: fill rates, lead times, inventory turns, on-time delivery — enabling data-driven decisions.
  • Financial integration: Every supply chain transaction (goods receipt, shipment, return) is automatically posted to accounts — eliminates separate finance reconciliation.

Q7a) What is E-commerce?   b) Advantages & Limitations   c) Threats of E-commerce   d) What is E-governance?15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) E-Commerce — Definition

E-Commerce (Electronic Commerce) is the buying and selling of goods, services, and information over electronic systems — primarily the internet. It encompasses the full spectrum of online commercial activity: browsing, ordering, payment, fulfilment, and after-sales service — all conducted digitally.

Scope: B2C (Amazon), B2B (Alibaba wholesale), C2C (eBay), G2C (government services), G2B (e-procurement). Transactions include physical goods, digital products, services, and financial instruments.

b) Advantages of E-Commerce

  • 24×7 availability: Customers can shop at any time — no store opening hours.
  • Global reach: A small business can sell to customers worldwide without physical expansion.
  • Lower cost: Reduced overhead vs physical stores — no rent, fewer staff, no in-store displays.
  • Personalisation: Recommendation engines, targeted promotions based on browsing history.
  • Wider product range: No shelf-space constraints — infinite virtual catalogue (long tail).
  • Price comparison: Customers easily compare prices across multiple vendors.
  • Faster transactions: Instant digital payments; digital products delivered immediately.
  • Data collection: Detailed analytics on customer behaviour enables better business decisions.

Limitations of E-Commerce

  • No physical inspection: Customers cannot touch, smell, or try products before buying — higher return rates.
  • Security concerns: Fraud, phishing, data breaches erode consumer trust.
  • Digital divide: Excludes populations without internet access or digital literacy.
  • Delivery delays: Physical goods still require shipping — cannot match instant in-store fulfilment for urgent needs.
  • Technology dependence: Server downtime means lost sales.
  • Last-mile challenges: Logistics to remote areas remains difficult and expensive.
  • Legal and regulatory complexity: Cross-border tax, customs, and consumer protection laws vary.

c) Threats of E-Commerce

  • Phishing: Fake websites/emails trick customers into revealing credentials or payment info.
  • Credit card fraud: Stolen card details used for unauthorised purchases.
  • DoS/DDoS attacks: Flooding servers with traffic to take the site offline.
  • SQL injection: Attackers inject malicious SQL into web forms to access/modify the database.
  • Man-in-the-middle (MITM): Attacker intercepts communication between browser and server.
  • Malware/ransomware: Infects e-commerce servers, steals data, or encrypts files for ransom.
  • Data breaches: Theft of customer PII and payment data (e.g., Target, Equifax breaches).
  • Fake reviews and counterfeit goods: Damage trust and brand reputation.
  • Price scraping and IP theft: Competitors scrape pricing and product data.

d) E-Governance

E-Governance is the application of ICT (Information and Communication Technology) by government agencies to deliver services to citizens (G2C), businesses (G2B), employees (G2E), and other government agencies (G2G) — improving efficiency, transparency, and accessibility.

Key benefits: Reduces corruption (no human intermediaries), 24×7 service access, lower administrative costs, faster processing, greater transparency and accountability.

Indian examples: Income Tax e-Filing portal, DigiLocker, UMANG app, IRCTC, GeM (Government e-Marketplace), Aadhaar-based services, e-Courts. See 2008-09 paper Q3 for full detail.

Q8a) Supply chain of E-commerce   b) Why SCM is vital   c) E-supply chain planning tools   d) SCM portals15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Supply Chain of E-Commerce

The e-commerce supply chain is the network of organisations, people, activities, information, and resources involved in moving a product from manufacturer to the end customer who ordered it online. It has unique challenges compared to traditional retail supply chains:

Supplier → Manufacturer → DC/Warehouse → [E-Commerce Platform]
                                              ↓
                                     Customer places order
                                              ↓
                               Pick-Pack in Fulfilment Centre
                                              ↓
                               Last-mile Carrier (courier)
                                              ↓
                                    Customer's Doorstep
  • Unique e-commerce SC characteristics: Very high order volumes with small quantities per order; extremely high speed expectations; complex last-mile delivery; high return rates; demand volatility (flash sales, seasonal spikes).
  • Fulfilment models: Own fulfilment centres (Amazon), drop-shipping (supplier ships direct), third-party logistics (3PL).

b) Why SCM is Vital for E-Commerce

  • Delivery is the product: In e-commerce, the customer experience is heavily defined by whether they get the right product on time. Supply chain execution IS the experience.
  • Speed as competitive weapon: Amazon's 2-hour/next-day delivery forces competitors to match — only excellent SCM enables this.
  • Inventory accuracy: Showing "In Stock" when out-of-stock destroys trust. SCM ensures what's shown online is what's available.
  • Cost control: Logistics costs are 10–30% of e-commerce revenue — efficient SCM directly determines profitability.
  • Scalability: E-commerce volumes can spike 10× overnight (Big Billion Day, Prime Day) — supply chain must scale.
  • Reverse logistics: High return rates (fashion: 30%+) require efficient return processing — supply chain function.
  • Supplier reliability: Stockouts kill sales and rankings on marketplaces — supplier SCM management is critical.

c) E-Supply Chain Planning Tools

  • Demand Forecasting: ML-based tools (Blue Yonder, SAP IBP) analyse historical sales, seasonal patterns, external signals to predict future demand by SKU/location.
  • Inventory Optimisation: Calculate optimal safety stock, reorder points, and order quantities across multi-echelon networks — balancing service level vs. inventory cost.
  • Supply Planning: Match sourcing capacity with demand forecasts — identifies supply gaps weeks/months ahead, enabling proactive action.
  • S&OP (Sales & Operations Planning): Consensus planning process bridging sales forecasts, operational capacity, and financial targets.
  • Network Design: Optimise DC locations, number of stocking points, and distribution lanes to minimise fulfilment cost and time.
  • Collaborative planning (CPFR): Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment — share forecasts with suppliers for joint planning.

d) How SCM Portals Help E-Commerce Manage Supply Chain

  • Supplier visibility portal: Suppliers log in to see open POs, confirm delivery dates, submit advance shipping notices — reduces chasing calls, improves schedule adherence.
  • Inventory visibility: All stakeholders see real-time stock levels across fulfilment centres — enables fast rebalancing of inventory.
  • Order tracking portal: Customers and distribution partners track shipments in real time with carrier integration — reduces "where is my order" (WISMO) calls.
  • Exception management: Portal surfaces delays, out-of-stock alerts, quality holds — teams act on exceptions proactively rather than reactively.
  • 3PL collaboration: 3PL warehouses and carriers connect to the portal to update status — single view of all fulfilment activity.
  • Analytics dashboard: Supply chain KPIs (fill rate, OTIF, inventory turns) visible to management — data-driven decisions.
Q9a) EDI & advantages   b) UN/EDIFACT   c) Firewall   d) Functions of Firewall15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) EDI — Definition and Advantages

EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) is the direct, computer-to-computer exchange of standardised business documents between trading partners — replacing paper-based processes with structured electronic messages.

Advantages of EDI:

  • Speed: Documents transmitted and processed in minutes vs. days for paper/postal methods.
  • Accuracy: Eliminates manual data entry errors — data flows system to system without human re-keying.
  • Cost savings: Reduces paper, postage, filing, data-entry labour, and storage costs.
  • Process automation: PO receipt triggers automatic stock check, acknowledgement, and production scheduling — no manual steps.
  • Better inventory management: Real-time order and delivery data enables JIT ordering and lower safety stock.
  • Improved trading partner relationships: Faster, more accurate transactions build trust; mandatory for major retailers.
  • Audit trail: All transactions electronically logged — easier compliance, dispute resolution, and audit.
  • Competitive necessity: Walmart, Tesco, Amazon all mandate EDI compliance from suppliers.

b) UN/EDIFACT

UN/EDIFACT (United Nations/Electronic Data Interchange for Administration, Commerce and Transport) is the international EDI standard developed under UN auspices, widely used in Europe, Asia, and for cross-border international trade.

  • Maintained by UNECE (United Nations Economic Commission for Europe).
  • Message hierarchy: Interchange (UNB/UNZ) → Functional Group (UNG/UNE) → Message (UNH/UNT) → Segment → Composite → Data Element.
  • Common EDIFACT messages: ORDERS (purchase order), ORDRSP (order response), DESADV (despatch advice), INVOIC (invoice), REMADV (remittance advice), CONTRL (acknowledgement), DELFOR (delivery forecast).
  • Character set: Uses delimiters (default: + for element separator, : for component separator, ' for segment terminator).
  • vs ANSI X12: X12 is the US domestic standard; EDIFACT is the international standard. Many companies support both.
Sample EDIFACT INVOIC segment:
UNB+UNOA:1+SENDER:1+RECEIVER:1+060101:1000+1'
UNH+1+INVOIC:D:01B:UN'
BGM+380+INV123+9'
DTM+137:20240101:102'
MOA+9:1500.00'
UNT+5+1'
UNZ+1+1'

c) Firewall — Definition

A firewall is a network security system — hardware appliance, software, or combination — that monitors and controls network traffic based on predefined security rules. It creates a barrier between a trusted internal network and untrusted external networks.

Types of firewalls:

  • Packet-filtering firewall: Inspects IP/TCP headers (source/destination IP, port, protocol) — allows or blocks based on ACL rules. Fast but cannot inspect packet content.
  • Stateful inspection firewall: Tracks connection state — ensures packets are part of legitimate established sessions. More secure than packet filtering.
  • Application proxy firewall: Acts as intermediary for specific applications (HTTP, FTP) — inspects content at application layer. Slow but very thorough.
  • Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW): Combines stateful inspection + deep packet inspection + IDS/IPS + application awareness.

d) Functions of Firewall in e-Commerce

  • Access control: Only allows traffic on port 443 (HTTPS) to the web server; blocks all other inbound connections.
  • DMZ isolation: Web server placed in DMZ — accessible from internet but isolated from internal payment and database servers.
  • IP blocking: Blocks known malicious IP ranges, Tor exit nodes, and geographic regions as needed.
  • DoS/DDoS protection: Rate-limits connections per IP; blocks SYN floods; triggers alerts on traffic spikes.
  • Content inspection: Application-layer firewall inspects HTTP requests for SQL injection patterns, XSS attempts, malformed requests.
  • VPN gateway: Authenticates and encrypts remote admin connections — prevents unauthorised admin access.
  • NAT: Hides internal network topology from external attackers — internal servers not directly reachable.
  • Logging & alerting: Records all sessions; generates alerts on policy violations — essential for security incident response and PCI-DSS compliance.
Q10a) ERP & why BPR before ERP   b) Reasons for ERP market growth & advantages15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) ERP — Definition and Why BPR is Required Before Implementation

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is an integrated suite of software applications that manages all core business processes — finance, HR, manufacturing, procurement, sales, supply chain — through a single unified database that all departments share in real time.

Why BPR must precede ERP:

  • ERP embeds best practices: ERP systems contain thousands of optimised business process templates built from industry best practices. If an organisation's processes are inefficient, simply automating them with ERP yields poor results — "a faster mess."
  • Reduces costly customisation: When processes align with ERP's built-in flows, customisation is minimal. Customising ERP heavily is expensive, error-prone, and makes upgrades very difficult.
  • Maximises ROI: BPR + ERP together deliver transformational gains; ERP alone on broken processes delivers modest improvement at high cost.
  • Change management: BPR forces staff to understand why processes must change → smoother ERP adoption.
  • Risk reduction: Many ERP failures (estimated 70% of large ERP projects fail or significantly underperform) occur because organisations tried to fit ERP around their old processes rather than redesigning processes to fit ERP.
  • Eliminates legacy complexity: BPR identifies and removes redundant processes before they get encoded in ERP configuration — cleaner, simpler ERP setup.

b) Reasons for ERP Market Growth

  • Globalisation: Multi-country operations demand integrated, standardised systems across geographies and currencies.
  • Y2K remediation: Late 1990s Y2K compliance push accelerated replacement of legacy systems with ERP.
  • Competitive pressure: Companies needed real-time visibility and lean operations to compete — ERP provides this.
  • Internet/e-commerce growth: Online channels require ERP integration for real-time inventory and order management.
  • Cloud ERP: SaaS ERP (SAP S/4HANA Cloud, Oracle NetSuite, Microsoft Dynamics 365) dramatically lowered cost and complexity — now accessible to SMEs.
  • Regulatory compliance: SOX, GST, GDPR, IFRS — all require integrated, auditable financial systems that ERP provides.
  • M&A integration: Mergers and acquisitions drive need to integrate multiple legacy systems onto one ERP platform.
  • Industry-specific solutions: ERP vendors now offer deep industry verticals (pharma, retail, automotive) — better fit drives adoption.

Advantages of ERP

  • Single source of truth: One database eliminates conflicting data across departments.
  • Real-time information: Management gets instant visibility into all operations.
  • Process standardisation: Best-practice processes enforced across all locations and divisions.
  • Reduced IT costs: One integrated system vs. dozens of separate siloed applications.
  • Better customer service: Sales can instantly check inventory, credit, and delivery status.
  • Improved financial control: Automated posting, period-end close, financial reporting, audit trail.
  • Scalability: Add modules, users, and geographies as business grows.
  • Supply chain optimisation: Integrated MRP, procurement, and logistics management.
  • Reduced cycle times: Automated workflows accelerate order-to-cash, procure-to-pay, hire-to-retire cycles.
Q11Short notes on any three: a) VPN   b) Digital Certificate   c) CRM   d) Data Warehouse   e) SCE Framework15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) VPN — Virtual Private Network

A VPN creates an encrypted, secure tunnel over a public network (internet), allowing private communication as if both parties were on the same local network. Full detail in 2008-09 paper Q11(e). Additional points:

  • In e-commerce: Secures admin access to e-commerce backend; enables secure B2B data exchange; allows remote staff to access ERP/payment systems securely.
  • SSL VPN vs IPSec VPN: SSL VPN accessible via browser (easier for remote users); IPSec VPN for site-to-site connections.
  • Split tunnelling: Only corporate traffic goes through VPN; personal browsing uses direct internet — reduces VPN load.

b) Digital Certificate

A Digital Certificate (also called Public Key Certificate or X.509 Certificate) is an electronic document issued by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) that binds a public key to an entity's identity.

  • Contents: Owner's name and details, owner's public key, CA's digital signature, validity period (not before/after dates), serial number, key usage restrictions.
  • How trust works: Browser trusts a root CA (pre-installed) → CA signs intermediate CA cert → intermediate CA signs website cert → chain of trust.
  • In e-commerce (HTTPS): When you visit https://amazon.com, your browser verifies Amazon's certificate — confirms you're talking to the real Amazon, not an impersonator.
  • In SET: All parties (cardholder, merchant, payment gateway) hold certificates — mutual authentication.
  • Types: DV (Domain Validation — just domain), OV (Organisation Validation — company verified), EV (Extended Validation — green bar, highest trust).

c) CRM — Customer Relationship Management

CRM is a strategy, process, and technology system for managing and analysing a company's interactions with current and potential customers throughout the customer lifecycle — with the goal of improving relationships, retaining customers, and driving sales growth.

  • Core modules: Sales force automation (SFA), marketing automation, customer service/helpdesk, analytics.
  • Key functions: Lead tracking, opportunity management, customer history, campaign management, case/ticket management, customer segmentation.
  • In e-commerce: CRM tracks purchase history, browsing behaviour, support tickets, and preferences → enables personalised marketing, targeted promotions, and proactive customer service.
  • Popular CRM systems: Salesforce, SAP CRM, HubSpot, Microsoft Dynamics CRM, Zoho CRM.
  • Benefits: Increased customer retention (5% increase → 25% profit increase), higher conversion rates, better cross-sell/upsell, reduced customer churn.

d) Data Warehouse

A Data Warehouse (DW) is a large, centralised repository that stores integrated, historical, and cleaned data from multiple operational systems (ERP, CRM, e-commerce) — optimised for analytical queries and reporting rather than transaction processing.

  • Characteristics (Bill Inmon's 4): Subject-oriented (organised by subject like sales, inventory), Integrated (data from multiple sources harmonised), Non-volatile (data doesn't change once loaded), Time-variant (stores historical data over time).
  • Components: ETL (Extract-Transform-Load) pipeline, DW storage layer (star/snowflake schema), OLAP cube, BI reporting tools.
  • In e-commerce: Consolidates sales data from website, stores, app, and marketplaces → analysts run complex reports: sales trends, customer lifetime value, product performance, regional analysis.
  • Examples: Amazon Redshift, Google BigQuery, Snowflake, SAP BW.

e) SCE Framework

See 2008-09 paper Q11(b) for full detail. Summary: Supply Chain Execution (SCE) is the operational layer that physically moves goods as planned by SCP (Supply Chain Planning). Key systems: WMS, TMS, OMS, MES. Bridges the planning world (forecasts, schedules) with the physical world (warehouse operations, trucking, delivery).
