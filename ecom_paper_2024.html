Q1(I)SET protocol is used for ________1M
✓ Answer

SET (Secure Electronic Transaction) protocol is used for securing credit card transactions over the internet. It was developed jointly by Visa and MasterCard to provide authentication of cardholders, merchants, and banks; confidentiality of payment data; and message integrity during online credit card payments. SET uses digital certificates, digital signatures, and encryption (DES + RSA) to ensure that card details are never exposed to the merchant — only the payment gateway sees them.

Q1(II)What type of ad appears on top of a web page?1M
✓ Answer

A Banner Ad appears on top of a web page. It is a rectangular graphic advertisement typically placed at the top (header) of a webpage — usually 728×90 pixels (leaderboard) or 970×90 pixels (large leaderboard). Banner ads display brand logos, promotional messages, and a call-to-action (CTA) link. They operate on CPM (cost per thousand impressions) or CPC (cost per click) pricing models. Common on news sites, portals, and content websites (e.g., Times of India, CNN).

Q1(III)What is the name of the card which can be used by buyers during purchase and in which the amount is immediately debited from the buyer's account?1M
✓ Answer

Debit Card. When a buyer uses a debit card for a purchase (online or POS), the transaction amount is immediately debited from the buyer's linked bank (savings/current) account — unlike a credit card where the bank pays first and the buyer pays later. The buyer can only spend up to their available bank balance. Examples: Visa Debit, MasterCard Debit, RuPay. In India, UPI also functions similarly — instantly debiting from the bank account.

Q1(IV)What is the name for direct computer-to-computer transfer of transaction information contained in standard business documents?1M
✓ Answer

EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) — the direct computer-to-computer exchange of standard business documents (purchase orders, invoices, advance ship notices, payment advices) between trading partners in a machine-readable, agreed format. No human intervention or paper documents are involved. Standards: ANSI X12 (North America), UN/EDIFACT (international). Communication: via VAN (Value Added Network) or AS2 over the internet.

Q1(V)What is e-business?1M
✓ Answer

E-business (Electronic Business) is the conduct of all business processes using internet and digital technologies — not just buying and selling (e-commerce), but the entire range of business activities: internal operations (ERP, HR, accounting), supply chain management, customer relationship management (CRM), knowledge management, and collaboration. E-commerce is a subset of e-business — e-business is the broader transformation of all business processes through technology.

Q1(VII)________ type of E-Commerce has trade and transaction dealings between business establishments.1M
✓ Answer

B2B (Business-to-Business) e-commerce has trade and transaction dealings between business establishments. In B2B, one business sells goods or services to another business — manufacturer to distributor, supplier to manufacturer, wholesaler to retailer. Characteristics: high-volume orders, negotiated pricing, credit terms, contract-based relationships, EDI/ERP integration. Examples: Alibaba, IndiaMART, Udaan, Amazon Business. B2B accounts for the largest share of e-commerce by transaction value (5–6× larger than B2C).

Q1(VIII)SCM is a game of 4 C's — Collaboration, Co-operation, Co-ordination and ________1M
✓ Answer

SCM is a game of 4 C's — Collaboration, Co-operation, Co-ordination and Communication. Effective Supply Chain Management requires all partners (suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, retailers) to communicate transparently — sharing demand forecasts, inventory levels, production schedules, and delivery status in real time. Without communication, the other three C's cannot function — information asymmetry leads to the bullwhip effect, excess inventory, and missed deliveries.

Q1(IX)What is the full form of CGI?1M
✓ Answer

CGI = Common Gateway Interface. CGI is a standard protocol that defines how a web server passes a user's request to an external application program and receives the output back to send to the user's browser. It allows web servers to execute server-side scripts (Perl, Python, C, Shell) to generate dynamic web pages — processing form submissions, database queries, and interactive content. CGI was the earliest method for creating dynamic websites before modern frameworks (PHP, Node.js, Django).

The purpose of SCM is to manage the flow of goods, information, and finances across the entire supply chain — from raw material sourcing to final delivery to the end customer — in the most efficient, cost-effective, and responsive manner. Goals: minimise total cost, reduce lead times, maintain optimal inventory levels, ensure product quality, maximise customer satisfaction, and build resilient, agile supply networks that can adapt to disruptions.

Q1(XI)What are the characteristics of Digital Cash?1M
✓ Answer

Characteristics of Digital Cash (e-Cash):

  • Monetary value: Represents real currency stored in digital form.
  • Exchangeability: Can be exchanged for physical cash or other digital payment forms.
  • Retrievability: Can be stored and retrieved from digital wallets or accounts.
  • Anonymity: Transactions can be made without revealing the identity of the payer (like physical cash).
  • Divisibility: Can be divided into smaller denominations for exact payments.
  • Non-forgeability: Cannot be duplicated or counterfeited due to cryptographic security.
  • Transferability: Can be transferred from one party to another electronically.

Q2What are digital signatures? How do they differ from digital certificates? [5]5 Marks
✓ Answer

Digital Signature

A digital signature is a cryptographic mechanism that proves the authenticity, integrity, and non-repudiation of a digital message or document. It is the electronic equivalent of a handwritten signature, but far more secure.

How it works:

Sender:
  1. Compute hash of the message: H = SHA-256(Message)
  2. Encrypt the hash with sender's PRIVATE key: Signature = H^D mod N
  3. Send: Message + Signature

Receiver:
  1. Decrypt the signature using sender's PUBLIC key: H' = Signature^E mod N
  2. Compute hash of received message: H = SHA-256(Received Message)
  3. If H == H' → Signature is VALID (authentic, untampered, non-repudiable)

Digital Certificate

A digital certificate is an electronic document issued by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) that binds a public key to the identity of its owner (person, organisation, or server). It contains: owner's name, public key, CA's name, serial number, validity period, and the CA's digital signature.

Q3What are the main disadvantages of E-Commerce? [5]5 Marks
✓ Answer

Despite its growth, e-commerce has several significant disadvantages:

  • Security risks: Online transactions expose customers to phishing, card fraud, data breaches, and identity theft. Even with SSL/TLS, attacks evolve constantly. Customer trust is fragile — one breach can destroy a brand.
  • Lack of physical experience: Customers cannot touch, feel, try on, or physically inspect products before purchase — leading to dissatisfaction, wrong size/colour orders, and high return rates (30–40% in fashion).
  • Delivery delays and logistics costs: Shipping takes time (unlike instant gratification of in-store purchase). Last-mile delivery in rural areas is expensive and unreliable. Returns logistics adds further cost.
  • Digital divide: Not everyone has internet access, smartphones, or digital literacy — excluding a significant population (especially elderly, rural, and economically weaker sections) from e-commerce benefits.
  • Intense competition and low margins: Low entry barriers mean thousands of competitors. Price transparency leads to price wars. Customer acquisition cost (via Google/Facebook ads) keeps rising, squeezing profit margins.
  • Technical infrastructure dependency: E-commerce is entirely dependent on internet connectivity, server uptime, and payment gateway availability. Any outage means zero revenue during that period.
  • Privacy concerns: E-commerce platforms collect vast amounts of personal data (browsing history, purchases, payment info, location). Misuse, sale to third parties, or inadequate protection erodes customer trust.
  • Legal and regulatory complexity: Different countries have different consumer protection, taxation (GST), data protection (GDPR, IT Act), and customs regulations — compliance is complex and costly for cross-border commerce.
  • Impersonal customer service: Chatbots and email cannot replicate the warmth and problem-solving of face-to-face interaction — customer grievance resolution can be frustrating online.

Q5Describe the advantages and disadvantages of E-marketing over normal marketing. [5]5 Marks
✓ Answer

Advantages of E-Marketing over Traditional Marketing

  • Global reach: E-marketing reaches billions of internet users worldwide — no geographic limitations. A small business in Kolkata can market to customers in the US, UK, or Australia.
  • Lower cost: Email campaigns, social media posts, and SEO cost a fraction of TV ads, print ads, and billboards. Google Ads allows budgets as low as ₹100/day.
  • Precise targeting: Digital platforms allow targeting by demographics (age, gender, location), interests, behaviour, purchase history, and lookalike audiences — minimising wasted ad spend.
  • Measurable results: Every click, impression, conversion, and sale is tracked in real time (Google Analytics, Meta Ads Manager). ROI is precisely measurable — unlike billboards or newspaper ads.
  • Personalisation: AI-driven recommendations, personalised emails, retargeting ads based on browsing history — each customer gets a tailored experience.
  • 24/7 availability: E-marketing campaigns run continuously — automated email sequences, social media scheduling, and always-on search ads work even while marketers sleep.
  • Two-way interaction: Social media allows direct customer engagement — comments, reviews, polls, live Q&A. Traditional marketing is one-way broadcast.

Disadvantages of E-Marketing

  • Digital divide: Not all target customers are online — elderly, rural, and low-income segments may be unreachable through digital channels.
  • Ad fatigue and ad-blocking: Users are bombarded with ads — banner blindness, ad-blockers, and spam filters reduce effectiveness. 40%+ of users use ad-blockers.
  • Privacy concerns: Tracking user behaviour (cookies, pixels) raises privacy issues. GDPR and similar regulations restrict data collection and targeting capabilities.
  • High competition: Low entry barriers mean everyone markets online — standing out requires significant creativity and budget in a crowded digital space.
  • Dependence on technology: Platform algorithm changes (Google, Facebook, Instagram) can drastically reduce organic reach overnight. Businesses are at the mercy of platform policies.
  • Negative publicity risk: A single negative review, viral complaint, or social media crisis can damage brand reputation rapidly and publicly.

  • EDI (Electronic Data Interchange): Automated exchange of business documents (POs, invoices, ASNs) between trading partners in standardised formats (ANSI X12, EDIFACT).

  • VPN (Virtual Private Network): Secure encrypted tunnels over the public internet — enables trading partners to access each other's extranets securely.
  • Digital Certificates & PKI: X.509 certificates for authenticating business entities in electronic transactions — ensuring you're transacting with the genuine partner.
  • Payment systems: B2B payment platforms supporting wire transfers, ACH, trade credit, letters of credit, and electronic invoicing.

Q7(a) Supply chain of E-commerce [2]   (b) Why SCM vital for E-commerce [3]   (c) E-supply chain planning tools [5]   (d) SCM portals for E-commerce [5]15 Marks
✓ Answer

(a) Supply Chain of E-Commerce [2]

The supply chain of e-commerce is the end-to-end network of organisations, processes, and technologies involved in moving products from raw material suppliers to the final consumer through digital channels. Unlike traditional retail supply chains (which end at a store shelf), e-commerce supply chains extend to the customer's doorstep:

Supplier → Manufacturer → Warehouse/Fulfilment Centre → Last-Mile Delivery → Customer's Doorstep
    ↑                                                                              │
    └──────────── Returns / Reverse Logistics ◄────────────────────────────────────┘

Key difference: e-commerce supply chains handle individual orders (each-picks) rather than bulk shipments to stores, and must manage last-mile delivery to millions of individual addresses.

(b) Why is SCM Vital for E-Commerce? [3]

  • Customer expectations of speed: Amazon, Flipkart, Blinkit have set expectations of same-day/next-day/10-minute delivery. Without efficient SCM, these delivery promises cannot be met — leading to customer churn.
  • Inventory optimisation: E-commerce catalogues list millions of products. SCM ensures the right products are stocked in the right fulfilment centres closest to demand centres — reducing shipping costs and delivery times.
  • Cost control: Logistics costs (warehousing + shipping + returns) can be 20–40% of e-commerce revenue. Efficient SCM reduces these costs through route optimisation, demand forecasting, and vendor-managed inventory.
  • Handling returns: E-commerce return rates are 20–40% (especially fashion). SCM must handle reverse logistics efficiently — picking up returns, inspecting, restocking or liquidating — without destroying profitability.
  • Competitive advantage: Companies with superior SCM (Amazon, Walmart) win on price, speed, and availability. SCM is the operational backbone that enables the customer experience.

(c) E-Supply Chain Planning Tools [5]

E-Supply Chain Planning (e-SCP) tools are software solutions that use data, algorithms, and digital connectivity to plan, optimise, and coordinate supply chain operations:

  • Demand Planning & Forecasting: Uses historical sales data, market trends, seasonality, promotions, and ML algorithms to predict future demand. Tools: SAP IBP, Oracle Demand Management, Blue Yonder. Accurate demand forecasting prevents both overstocking (carrying cost) and understocking (lost sales).
  • Supply Planning: Determines how to meet forecasted demand — sourcing decisions, supplier allocation, make-vs-buy decisions, lead time planning. Balances supply capacity with demand across multiple suppliers and locations.
  • Inventory Optimisation: Algorithms determine optimal inventory levels, safety stock, reorder points, and allocation across multiple warehouses/fulfilment centres. Multi-echelon inventory optimisation (MEIO) considers the entire network.
  • Production Planning & Scheduling: Plans production runs, material requirements (MRP), machine scheduling, and capacity planning. Ensures manufacturing output aligns with demand forecasts and order commitments.
  • Transportation Planning & Route Optimisation: Plans shipment routes, carrier selection, load consolidation, and delivery scheduling. Tools use GPS data and algorithms to find optimal routes — minimising cost and delivery time.
  • CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting, and Replenishment): A framework where retailers and suppliers share forecasts, sales data, and inventory levels electronically — jointly planning replenishment to reduce the bullwhip effect.

(d) SCM Portals for E-Commerce [5]

Supply Chain Management portals are web-based platforms that provide a single digital interface for all supply chain participants to collaborate, share information, and execute transactions:

  • Supplier Portal: Suppliers log in to view purchase orders, confirm delivery schedules, submit invoices, update product catalogues, and check payment status. Eliminates email/phone communication delays. Examples: SAP Ariba Supplier Network, Coupa.
  • Procurement Portal: Buyers search supplier catalogues, raise purchase requisitions, get approvals, issue POs, and manage contracts — all through a single web interface. RFQ (Request for Quote) and e-auction features enable competitive sourcing.
  • Logistics/Tracking Portal: Real-time visibility into shipment status, warehouse inventory levels, carrier performance, and delivery confirmations. Both internal teams and customers can track orders. Integration with carrier APIs (FedEx, DHL, Delhivery).
  • Order Management Portal: Central hub for managing all orders across channels (website, marketplace, mobile app). Handles order routing to the nearest fulfilment centre, inventory allocation, split shipments, and order status updates.
  • Returns Portal: Customers initiate returns online — select reason, schedule pickup, track refund status. Internally, the portal manages quality inspection, restocking, refurbishment, or disposal workflows.

Benefits: Real-time visibility, reduced information asymmetry, faster decision-making, automated workflows, lower communication costs, improved supplier relationships, and better customer experience.

(a) Firewall and Its Functions in E-Commerce [5]

A firewall is a network security device (hardware, software, or both) that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules. It acts as a barrier between a trusted internal network and untrusted external networks (the internet).

   INTERNET (Untrusted)          FIREWALL          INTERNAL NETWORK (Trusted)
  ┌─────────────────┐      ┌───────────────┐      ┌──────────────────┐
  │  Hackers        │      │               │      │  Web Server      │
  │  Malware        │─────►│  Rules Engine  │─────►│  App Server      │
  │  DDoS attacks   │      │  Packet Filter │      │  Database Server │
  │  Legitimate     │      │  Stateful      │      │  Payment Gateway │
  │  users          │      │  Inspection    │      │  Employee Systems│
  └─────────────────┘      └───────────────┘      └──────────────────┘
                           ALLOW ✓ or BLOCK ✗

Types of Firewalls:

  • Packet-filtering firewall: Examines each packet's header (source/destination IP, port, protocol) and allows/blocks based on rules. Fast but cannot inspect packet content.
  • Stateful inspection firewall: Tracks the state of active connections — ensures incoming packets belong to legitimate, established sessions. More secure than simple packet filtering.
  • Application-level gateway (proxy firewall): Inspects the actual content of application-layer traffic (HTTP, FTP, SMTP). Can block specific URLs, filter web content, and detect malicious payloads.
  • Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW): Combines all above + deep packet inspection, intrusion prevention (IPS), SSL/TLS inspection, and application awareness.

Functions in E-Commerce:

  • Protect customer data (credit cards, PAN, addresses) from external attackers
  • Block SQL injection, XSS, and other web application attacks (WAF — Web Application Firewall)
  • Prevent DDoS attacks from taking the e-commerce site offline
  • Control which internal systems can communicate with the internet (DMZ architecture)
  • Log all traffic for audit and compliance (PCI DSS requires firewall for card processing)
  • Enforce access control — only authorised IPs can access admin panels and databases

(b) Working Principle of RSA Algorithm [5]

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) — Asymmetric Encryption Algorithm

KEY GENERATION:
  1. Select two large prime numbers: P and Q
  2. Compute modulus:   N = P × Q
  3. Compute totient:   φ(N) = (P−1) × (Q−1)
  4. Choose public exponent E:
        1 < E < φ(N)  and  gcd(E, φ(N)) = 1
  5. Compute private exponent D:
        E × D ≡ 1 (mod φ(N))
        [Solve using Extended Euclidean Algorithm]

  Public key  : (E, N)   ← share freely
  Private key : (D, N)   ← keep secret

ENCRYPTION (sender uses recipient's PUBLIC key):
  Ciphertext C = M^E mod N

DECRYPTION (recipient uses their PRIVATE key):
  Plaintext  M = C^D mod N

DIGITAL SIGNATURE:
  hash = SHA-256(Message)
  Signature = hash^D mod N
  [Verification: hash^E mod N == SHA-256(received message)]

WORKED EXAMPLE (P=11, Q=13):
  N    = 11 × 13 = 143
  φ(N) = 10 × 12 = 120
  E    = 7        [gcd(7, 120) = 1 ✓]
  D    = 103      [7 × 103 = 721 = 6×120+1 → 721 mod 120 = 1 ✓]

  Encrypt M=9:  C = 9^7 mod 143 = 4782969 mod 143 = 48
  Decrypt C=48: M = 48^103 mod 143 = 9 ✓

SECURITY:
  Breaking RSA = factoring N into P and Q
  For N ≥ 2048 bits → computationally infeasible
  with classical computers

Q9(a) What is ERP? Why BPR before ERP? [4]   (b) Reasons for ERP market growth [5]   (c) Advantages of ERP [6]15 Marks
✓ Answer

(a) ERP and Why BPR is Required Before ERP Implementation [4]

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is an integrated software system that manages and automates all core business processes of an organisation — finance, HR, manufacturing, supply chain, sales, procurement, CRM — in a single unified database and platform.

ERP modules: Finance & Accounting, Human Resources, Supply Chain Management, Manufacturing/Production, Sales & Distribution, Procurement, CRM, Project Management. Examples: SAP S/4HANA, Oracle ERP Cloud, Microsoft Dynamics 365.

Why BPR (Business Process Re-engineering) Before ERP:

BPR is the fundamental rethinking and radical redesign of business processes to achieve dramatic improvements in cost, quality, speed, and service. BPR must be done before ERP implementation because:

  • Eliminate inefficient processes: Automating a broken process only speeds up the wrong thing. BPR removes redundant, unnecessary, and wasteful steps before coding them into ERP.
  • Align with best practices: ERP software is designed around industry best practices. If existing processes are non-standard, they won't fit the ERP — either the ERP needs expensive customisation (risky) or processes must change (BPR).
  • Reduce customisation: Companies that skip BPR end up heavily customising ERP to match existing (often outdated) processes — making upgrades difficult, expensive, and error-prone.
  • Change management: BPR prepares the organisation for change — employees understand why processes are changing before the software arrives. Without BPR, employees resist the new system.
  • Data cleanup: BPR identifies what data is needed and standardises master data — ensuring clean, consistent data migration into ERP rather than migrating garbage data.

(b) Reasons for Growth of ERP Market [5]

  • Need for integration: Companies operating with disconnected systems (separate software for finance, HR, inventory, sales) face data silos, duplication, and inconsistency. ERP integrates everything into one system — creating a single source of truth.
  • Cloud computing: Cloud-based ERP (SaaS) has dramatically lowered the entry barrier — no upfront hardware investment, subscription-based pricing, faster deployment. SMEs can now afford ERP (Zoho, ERPNext, SAP Business One).
  • Globalisation: Companies operating across countries need multi-currency, multi-language, multi-tax-regime capabilities — ERP provides this. Global supply chains demand integrated visibility.
  • Regulatory compliance: Increasing regulations (GST in India, SOX in US, GDPR in EU) require accurate, auditable financial records and data management — ERP ensures compliance with built-in controls.
  • Data-driven decision making: Modern ERP systems include analytics, dashboards, and AI — providing real-time business intelligence. Management can make informed decisions based on accurate, up-to-date data.
  • Digital transformation: Companies across all industries are digitising operations — ERP is the backbone of digital transformation, connecting front-office (CRM, e-commerce) with back-office (finance, supply chain).
  • Competitive pressure: Competitors using ERP gain efficiency advantages — companies without ERP face cost and speed disadvantages, forcing adoption.
  • Industry-specific solutions: ERP vendors now offer industry-specific solutions (automotive, pharma, retail, construction) — making adoption relevant and valuable for niche industries.

(c) Advantages of ERP System [6]

  • Single source of truth: All departments access the same real-time data from one database — eliminating data silos, duplicate entries, and conflicting information.
  • Process automation: Automates repetitive tasks — purchase order generation, invoice matching, payroll processing, financial reporting — reducing manual effort and errors.
  • Improved efficiency: Streamlined workflows across departments reduce cycle times — order-to-cash, procure-to-pay, and hire-to-retire processes become faster and leaner.
  • Better decision making: Real-time dashboards, analytics, and reports provide management with accurate, up-to-date business intelligence — enabling data-driven strategic and operational decisions.
  • Cost reduction: Reduced inventory carrying costs (better demand planning), lower procurement costs (better supplier management), reduced IT costs (one system instead of many), and fewer errors (less rework).
  • Regulatory compliance: Built-in compliance controls for tax (GST), financial reporting (GAAP/IFRS), data protection, and industry regulations. Automatic audit trails for every transaction.
  • Scalability: ERP systems grow with the business — adding new users, modules, geographies, and business units without replacing the core system.
  • Enhanced collaboration: Shared platform enables cross-departmental collaboration — sales can see inventory levels, finance can see outstanding purchase orders, HR can see project costs.
  • Customer satisfaction: Faster order processing, accurate delivery commitments, and responsive customer service (CRM integration) — leading to improved customer experience and loyalty.
  • Supply chain visibility: End-to-end visibility from procurement to delivery — enabling proactive management of delays, shortages, and quality issues.
Q10(a) Call center and equipment [5]   (b) Modes of operation [5]   (c) CPE and Denial of Service attacks [5]15 Marks
✓ Answer

(a) Call Center and Call Center Equipment [5]

A call center is a centralised facility used by organisations to receive and transmit a large volume of telephone calls from customers — handling enquiries, complaints, technical support, sales, and other customer interaction services. In e-commerce, call centers handle order queries, delivery issues, returns, and payment problems.

Types:

  • Inbound call center: Receives calls from customers — support, enquiries, complaints, order tracking.
  • Outbound call center: Makes calls to customers — telemarketing, sales, surveys, payment reminders, follow-ups.
  • Blended call center: Handles both inbound and outbound calls.
  • Virtual call center: Agents work remotely from different locations — connected via internet and cloud telephony.

Call Center Equipment:

  • ACD (Automatic Call Distributor): Routes incoming calls to the most appropriate available agent based on skills, language, queue length, and priority. The core technology of any call center.
  • IVR (Interactive Voice Response): Automated phone menu system — "Press 1 for order status, Press 2 for returns..." Handles simple queries without human agents, reducing wait times and costs.
  • PBX (Private Branch Exchange): Internal telephone switching system that manages all internal and external calls — connects agents' headsets to external PSTN or VoIP networks.
  • CTI (Computer Telephony Integration): Links telephone system with CRM software — when a customer calls, their account details, order history, and previous interactions automatically appear on the agent's screen (screen pop).
  • Predictive Dialer: For outbound calls — automatically dials multiple numbers simultaneously using algorithms, connects agents only to answered calls — maximising talk time and productivity.
  • Call Recording System: Records all calls for quality assurance, training, dispute resolution, and compliance.
  • Headsets: Noise-cancelling headsets with microphones — essential equipment for agents to communicate clearly in a noisy environment.
  • CRM Software: Salesforce, Freshdesk, Zendesk — tracks all customer interactions, ticket history, and resolution status across channels (phone, email, chat).
  • Workforce Management (WFM) Software: Forecasts call volumes by hour/day, schedules agent shifts, monitors adherence, and manages agent performance metrics.

(b) Modes of Operation of Call Center [5]

  • Inbound mode: The call center receives calls initiated by customers. Agents answer queries, resolve complaints, provide technical support, process orders, and handle escalations. Key metrics: Average Speed of Answer (ASA), First Call Resolution (FCR), Customer Satisfaction (CSAT).
  • Outbound mode: The call center initiates calls to customers or prospects. Activities include telemarketing (selling products/services), lead generation, customer surveys, appointment scheduling, payment collection reminders, and proactive service notifications. Key metrics: Conversion rate, calls per hour, talk time.
  • Blended mode: Agents handle both inbound and outbound calls — when inbound volume is low, agents make outbound calls; when inbound spikes, outbound campaigns pause. ACD system manages the blending dynamically.
  • Multichannel / Omnichannel mode: Modern call centers handle not just phone calls but also email, live chat, social media (Twitter, Facebook), WhatsApp, and video calls — all from a unified agent desktop. Customer can start on chat and escalate to phone without repeating information.
  • Self-service mode (IVR-based): Customers interact with automated IVR systems to get information (order status, account balance, refund status) or perform actions (activate SIM, pay bill) without speaking to a human agent. Reduces cost and wait times.

(c) Customer Premises Equipment (CPE) and Denial of Service Attacks [5]

CPE (Customer Premises Equipment):

CPE refers to any terminal equipment, network device, or hardware located at the customer's premises that connects to a service provider's network. It sits on the customer's side of the network demarcation point.

  • Examples: Broadband modem/router (Jio Fiber ONT, Airtel DSL modem), Wi-Fi router (TP-Link, D-Link), IP phone (VoIP desk phone), set-top box (DTH box), POS terminal (payment terminal at shop), corporate firewall/router, smart electricity meter.
  • May be owned by customer or provided/leased by ISP/telecom
  • Customer is responsible for operation and maintenance

Denial of Service (DoS) Attack:

A DoS (Denial of Service) attack is a cyberattack that aims to make a computer, server, or network resource unavailable to its intended users by overwhelming it with a flood of illegitimate requests — exhausting bandwidth, CPU, memory, or connection capacity.

  • DoS: Attack from a single source — sending massive traffic to overwhelm the target. Easier to mitigate (block the attacking IP).
  • DDoS (Distributed DoS): Attack from thousands/millions of compromised devices (botnets) simultaneously — much harder to mitigate because traffic comes from many different IPs worldwide.

Types of DoS/DDoS attacks:

  • Volumetric attacks: Flood the network bandwidth — UDP flood, ICMP flood, DNS amplification. Measured in Gbps.
  • Protocol attacks: Exploit weaknesses in network protocols — SYN flood (exhaust connection table), Ping of Death. Measured in packets per second.
  • Application layer attacks: Target the web application — HTTP flood, Slowloris (keeps connections open). Hardest to detect as requests look legitimate.

Impact on E-Commerce:

DDoS takes the e-commerce website offline → customers cannot browse/purchase → direct revenue loss. During peak seasons (Diwali, Black Friday), even minutes of downtime cost crores. Mitigation: CDN (Cloudflare, Akamai), rate limiting, traffic scrubbing, WAF, and redundant server architecture.

Q11(a) What is EDI? [1]   (b) Basic components [4]   (c) Role in E-commerce [4]   (d) EDIFACT [4]   (e) Benefits [2]15 Marks
✓ Answer

(a) What is EDI? [1]

EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) is the computer-to-computer exchange of standard business documents (purchase orders, invoices, shipping notices, payment advices) between trading partner organisations in a structured, machine-readable, agreed-upon format — without manual intervention or paper documents.

(b) Basic Components of EDI [4]

  • EDI Standards: Define the format and structure of business documents. Two main standards:
    • ANSI X12: Developed by ANSI (American National Standards Institute) — widely used in North America. Document types identified by numbers (e.g., 850 = Purchase Order, 810 = Invoice, 856 = ASN).
    • UN/EDIFACT: Developed by the UN — used internationally (especially Europe, Asia). Uses message type codes (ORDERS, INVOIC, DESADV).
  • EDI Translation Software: Converts data from the internal application format (ERP/database) into the EDI standard format for outbound messages, and converts received EDI messages back into internal format. Acts as the "translator" between internal systems and EDI standards. Examples: IBM Sterling Integrator, OpenText Trading Grid, Cleo Integration Cloud.
  • Communication Network: The transport mechanism for transmitting EDI messages between trading partners:
    • VAN (Value Added Network): Third-party private network that acts as an electronic post office — stores and forwards EDI messages; provides mailbox services, audit trail, and protocol conversion.
    • AS2 (Applicability Statement 2): Modern alternative — sends EDI messages directly over the internet via HTTPS. Eliminates VAN fees; uses digital certificates for security.
    • SFTP, FTPS: Secure file transfer protocols used for batch EDI document exchange.
  • Internal Business Application: The ERP, order management system, or accounting software that generates and consumes business data. EDI integrates with these applications via APIs, database connectors, or flat file exchange. The application is the source (outbound PO) and destination (received invoice) of EDI data.
  • Trading Partner Agreement (TPA): A formal agreement between partners specifying: which document types to exchange, which EDI standard and version, communication protocol, response time requirements, acknowledgement rules (FA 997), and escalation procedures.

(c) Role of EDI in E-Commerce [4]

  • Automated order processing: When a customer places an order on an e-commerce site, the system can automatically generate an EDI 850 (Purchase Order) to the supplier — supplier's system receives it, creates a sales order, and triggers fulfilment without manual data entry.
  • Supply chain speed: EDI eliminates manual document creation, mailing, and re-keying — reducing order processing time from days to minutes. Critical for same-day/next-day delivery commitments.
  • Inventory visibility: EDI 846 (Inventory Inquiry/Advice) allows real-time sharing of inventory levels between e-commerce platforms and suppliers — preventing overselling and enabling accurate "In Stock" / "Out of Stock" status on the website.
  • Shipping and tracking: Suppliers send EDI 856 (Advance Ship Notice) with shipment details and tracking numbers — the e-commerce platform automatically updates the customer's order status and sends tracking notifications.
  • Invoice and payment automation: EDI 810 (Invoice) and EDI 820 (Payment Order) automate the financial settlement process — three-way matching (PO + receipt + invoice) happens automatically, enabling prompt payment and accurate accounting.
  • Error reduction: Manual data entry error rate is 2–5%; EDI error rate is near zero — reducing order errors, shipping mistakes, and billing disputes that damage customer experience and increase costs.
  • Cost reduction: Eliminates paper, postage, manual labour for document processing — studies show EDI saves $5–$30 per transaction compared to paper-based processing.
  • Marketplace compliance: Large e-commerce platforms (Amazon, Walmart) require suppliers to be EDI-capable — EDI is mandatory for doing business with major retailers.

(d) EDIFACT [4]

EDIFACT (Electronic Data Interchange for Administration, Commerce, and Transport) is an international EDI standard developed by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE). It is the most widely used EDI standard globally (outside North America where ANSI X12 dominates).

Structure of an EDIFACT message:

UNA:+.? '                    ← Service String Advice (delimiters)
UNB+UNOC:3+SENDER+RECEIVER+  ← Interchange Header
  240630:1200+REF001'
UNH+1+ORDERS:D:96A:UN'       ← Message Header (ORDERS = Purchase Order)
BGM+220+PO-12345+9'          ← Beginning of Message
DTM+137:20240630:102'        ← Date/Time (order date)
NAD+BY+BUYER-ID::91'         ← Name and Address (Buyer)
NAD+SE+SELLER-ID::91'        ← Name and Address (Seller)
LIN+1++ITEM-001:IN'          ← Line Item
QTY+21:500'                  ← Quantity (500 units)
PRI+AAA:25.00'               ← Price (₹25.00 per unit)
UNS+S'                       ← Section Control
CNT+2:1'                     ← Control Total
UNT+12+1'                    ← Message Trailer
UNZ+1+REF001'                ← Interchange Trailer

Usage: EDIFACT is mandatory for international trade, customs declarations (CUSDEC), shipping (IFTMIN), and cross-border B2B commerce. Administered by the UN/CEFACT body, with regular version updates.

(e) Benefits of EDI [2]

  • Speed: Documents transmitted in seconds vs. days for postal mail — orders processed in minutes instead of days.
  • Accuracy: Eliminates manual data entry errors — reducing order mistakes, billing disputes, and shipping errors.
  • Cost savings: Saves $5–$30 per transaction — no paper, printing, postage, or manual data entry labour costs.
  • Efficiency: Automated document exchange enables straight-through processing — PO received → sales order created → shipment triggered → invoice generated — without human intervention.
  • Better relationships: Faster, more accurate transactions improve trading partner relationships — fewer disputes, timely payments, and smoother collaboration.
  • Audit trail: Every EDI transaction is logged with timestamps — complete audit trail for compliance, dispute resolution, and business intelligence.
