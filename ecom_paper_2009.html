Q1(i)Symmetric encryption involves1M
a) Public key
b) Private key
c) Both of these
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (b)

Symmetric encryption uses the same single secret (private) key for both encryption and decryption. Both sender and receiver must possess and keep this key secret. Examples: DES, AES, 3DES. It does not involve public keys — that's asymmetric encryption.

Q1(vii)In RSA algorithm ________ numbers are involved.1M
a) Odd
b) Even
c) Prime
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (c) — Prime

RSA security is founded on the mathematical difficulty of factoring the product of two large prime numbers. The key generation starts: choose two large primes P and Q → compute N = P×Q → compute φ(N) = (P−1)(Q−1) → choose E and compute D. The strength of RSA depends entirely on P and Q being very large primes.

Q2Describe the impact of EDI on the Supply Chain Management of a manufacturing plant.5 Marks
✓ Answer

EDI transforms SCM in a manufacturing plant by replacing paper-based, manual processes with instant, automated electronic data exchange with suppliers and customers:

  • Automated procurement: When the plant's ERP detects stock below reorder level, it automatically generates and sends an EDI Purchase Order to the supplier — no manual intervention, no delay.
  • Advance Ship Notice (ASN): Supplier sends an EDI DESADV (despatch advice) before goods are shipped → plant can pre-schedule receiving dock, labour, and storage — no waiting at the loading bay.
  • Reduced lead times: EDI order-to-acknowledgement is minutes vs days for paper POs → production can be planned tighter, reducing work-in-progress inventory.
  • Just-In-Time (JIT) support: Real-time supplier communication enables JIT delivery — raw materials arrive exactly when needed → lower storage costs.
  • Invoice reconciliation: EDI invoice (INVOIC) is matched automatically against the PO and receipt note → invoice processing time drops from weeks to hours → better supplier relationships and early-payment discounts.
  • Reduced errors: No manual re-keying between systems → fewer wrong shipments, fewer disputes, lower rework costs.
  • Forecast sharing: EDI DELFOR (delivery forecast) messages share production schedules with suppliers → suppliers can plan capacity proactively.

Net result: Shorter order cycle, lower inventory levels, improved on-time delivery, and reduced SCM operating costs.

Q3What is supplier centric B2B electronic commerce?5 Marks
✓ Answer

Supplier-centric B2B e-commerce is a model where a single supplier sets up and operates the e-commerce platform, and multiple buyers come to that supplier's website/portal to browse catalogues, place orders, and conduct transactions.

Key Characteristics

  • The supplier controls the marketplace — catalogue, pricing, terms.
  • Buyers visit the supplier's portal (e.g., Dell.com for corporate PC purchasing).
  • The supplier provides punchout catalogues that integrate with buyers' procurement systems.
  • Suitable when one supplier is dominant or has a very wide product range.

Advantages

  • Supplier has full control over product presentation, pricing, and upselling.
  • Lower transaction costs — buyers can self-service 24×7.
  • Supplier can personalise the experience for each buyer (contract pricing, custom catalogues).
  • Direct relationship maintained — no marketplace fees or intermediaries.

Disadvantages

  • Buyers must visit multiple supplier sites if they source from many vendors — inefficient.
  • No price comparison across suppliers.
  • High setup and maintenance cost for the supplier.

Examples: Cisco Connection Online (CCO), Dell Premier, Grainger.com for MRO supplies.

Contrast with buyer-centric B2B: In buyer-centric, a large buyer (e.g., Walmart) sets up the portal and forces suppliers to connect to it.

Q4What is meant by technological convergence? What are its implications?5 Marks
✓ Answer

Technological convergence is the trend by which different technologies, media, and industries merge into a single device, platform, or service as they all digitise and move onto common networks (especially the internet).

Examples of Convergence

  • A smartphone converges: phone, camera, GPS, music player, computer, TV, bank.
  • VoIP converges: traditional telephony with internet data networks.
  • Smart TV converges: television, web browser, streaming services, gaming.
  • E-commerce converges: retail, banking (payments), logistics, and media.

Implications

  • Industry disruption: Traditional industry boundaries collapse — telecoms, media, IT, and retail compete in the same space.
  • New business models: Subscription streaming (Netflix), super-apps (WeChat), digital payments embedded in retail.
  • Consumer convenience: One device/platform for all needs — enhanced user experience.
  • Regulatory challenges: Existing regulations designed for separate industries (telecom, broadcasting, banking) struggle to govern converged entities.
  • Security risks: More connected devices = larger attack surface for cyber threats.
  • Digital divide: Those without access to converged technology are further disadvantaged.
  • Job displacement: Traditional industries (print media, physical retail) disrupted — workforce restructuring needed.

Q6Describe how RSA and DES algorithms can be used for EDI security.5 Marks
✓ Answer

EDI messages carry sensitive business data (prices, order volumes, payment details) and must be protected. RSA and DES are used together in a hybrid encryption scheme:

DES for EDI Data Encryption

  • DES (Data Encryption Standard) is a symmetric block cipher — fast and efficient for encrypting large EDI message bodies.
  • A random session key (DES key) is generated for each EDI transmission.
  • The EDI message (INVOIC, ORDERS etc.) is encrypted using this DES session key.
  • DES encrypts the data at high speed — suitable for large EDI files.

RSA for Key Exchange

  • RSA is used to encrypt the DES session key using the recipient's public RSA key.
  • The encrypted DES key is sent along with the DES-encrypted EDI message.
  • Only the recipient (who holds the matching RSA private key) can decrypt the DES key, and then use it to decrypt the EDI message.

RSA for Digital Signature (Authentication)

  • The sender computes a hash (MD5/SHA) of the EDI message.
  • Encrypts the hash with their RSA private key → digital signature.
  • Recipient verifies signature with sender's public key — ensures authenticity and non-repudiation.
Sender:
  EDI Message ──[DES session key]──→ Encrypted Message
  DES session key ──[Recipient RSA Public Key]──→ Encrypted Key
  Both sent together →→→

Receiver:
  Encrypted Key ──[Own RSA Private Key]──→ DES session key
  Encrypted Message ──[DES session key]──→ EDI Message

This hybrid approach gives the speed of symmetric encryption with the key-management security of asymmetric encryption.

Q7a) Firewall & functions   b) Digital Signature vs Certificate   c) RSA p=17, q=29   d) BPR helps ERP implementation15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Firewall — Definition and Functions in e-Commerce

A firewall is a network security device or software that monitors, filters, and controls network traffic between a trusted internal network and an untrusted external network based on security rules.

Functions in e-Commerce:

  • Packet filtering: Inspects IP headers — allows port 443 (HTTPS) to the web server; blocks all other inbound traffic.
  • Stateful inspection: Tracks connection state — ensures only responses to legitimate outbound requests are allowed in.
  • Application proxy: Acts as intermediary for HTTP — inspects content for malicious payloads (SQL injection, XSS attempts).
  • NAT (Network Address Translation): Hides internal IP addresses from the internet — prevents direct attacks on internal servers.
  • DMZ creation: Separates public web server from internal payment and database servers.
  • VPN termination: Provides secure remote access for admins and B2B partners.
  • Logging: Records all sessions for security audit and compliance.

Check: 3 × 299 = 897 = 2×448 + 1 → 897 mod 448 = 1 ✓

Encrypt message M:   C = M^E mod N = M^3 mod 493
Decrypt ciphertext C: M = C^D mod N = C^299 mod 493

d) "A proper BPR exercise helps in ERP implementation" — Comment

This statement is absolutely true. Here's why:

  • ERP embeds best practices: ERP systems like SAP have thousands of best-practice processes built in. If a company's existing processes are inefficient and bloated, simply automating them in ERP perpetuates the inefficiency ("automating a mess creates a faster mess").
  • BPR aligns processes to ERP: By redesigning processes before implementation, the company's workflows align with ERP's built-in processes → less customisation needed → lower implementation cost and risk.
  • Reduces ERP customisation: Heavy customisation of ERP to fit broken processes is extremely expensive and creates problems during upgrades. BPR eliminates the need for much of this customisation.
  • Manages resistance to change: BPR creates awareness of process problems → staff are more receptive to ERP changes as they understand the "why".
  • Improves ROI: Companies that do BPR before ERP see dramatically better ROI — the new processes + ERP automation compound the benefits.
  • Risk reduction: Many ERP failures happen because processes were not redesigned — the new system could not handle the old, unstructured ways of working.
Q8a) E-cash & how it works   b) Security requirements for e-payments   c) Why supply chain is vital   d) E-supply chain planning tools15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) E-Cash — What it is and How it Works

E-Cash (Electronic Cash / Digital Cash) is a digital form of money — cryptographically signed tokens representing monetary value — stored in a customer's e-wallet software on their computer or device. It aims to replicate the anonymity and convenience of physical cash online.

Steps in e-cash operation:

1. WITHDRAWAL:
   Customer requests e-cash from their bank via internet.
   Bank debits customer's account and issues digitally signed
   "coin" tokens (blind signature ensures bank can't trace later).
   Coins stored in customer's e-wallet software.

2. PAYMENT:
   Customer purchases on merchant's website.
   E-wallet software sends appropriate coin tokens to merchant.
   Transaction is instant, like handing over cash.

3. DEPOSIT:
   Merchant sends received coins to their bank.
   Bank verifies coins (checks digital signatures, checks
   against spent-coin DB to prevent double-spending).
   Bank credits merchant's account.

4. SETTLEMENT:
   Interbank settlement happens between customer's bank
   and merchant's bank — customer's bank pays merchant's bank.

b) Security Requirements for Safe E-Payments

  • Authentication: Both buyer and seller must prove their identity — digital certificates, passwords, biometrics.
  • Confidentiality: Payment details (card numbers, bank details) must be encrypted in transit — SSL/TLS (HTTPS).
  • Integrity: Data must not be altered in transit — hash functions / digital signatures detect tampering.
  • Non-repudiation: Neither party can deny a completed transaction — digital signatures provide proof.
  • Availability: Payment systems must be available 24×7 — redundancy, failover, DDoS protection.
  • Privacy: Customer financial data must not be shared without consent — PCI-DSS compliance.
  • Authorisation: Only the account owner can initiate payments — OTP, 2FA, 3D Secure.

c) Why Supply Chain is Vital for E-Commerce

  • Customer expectation: Online shoppers expect fast, accurate, trackable delivery — supply chain must deliver on this promise or the e-commerce business fails.
  • Competitive advantage: Amazon's 1-day delivery is a supply chain advantage, not just a technology one.
  • Inventory management: E-commerce sells across geographies — efficient supply chain ensures right products at right locations without excess inventory.
  • Cost control: Logistics is one of the biggest costs in e-commerce — optimised supply chain directly impacts profitability.
  • Returns management: High return rates in online retail require efficient reverse logistics — part of supply chain.
  • Supplier reliability: Out-of-stock products kill conversion rates — supply chain reliability keeps products available.

d) E-Supply Chain Planning Tools

  • Demand Planning tools: Use historical sales data, trends, and ML to forecast demand accurately → prevent overstock/stockouts.
  • Supply Planning tools: Match supply capacity with forecasted demand — raw material procurement planning, supplier capacity planning.
  • Inventory Optimisation: Calculate optimal reorder points, safety stock levels, and EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) across distribution network.
  • S&OP (Sales & Operations Planning): Aligns sales forecasts with production and supply plans — cross-functional consensus planning.
  • Network Design tools: Model optimal warehouse/DC locations to minimise shipping cost and delivery time.
  • Examples: SAP APO (Advanced Planning & Optimisation), Oracle SCM Cloud, JDA (now Blue Yonder), i2 Technologies.
Q9a) ERP & its relation to e-commerce   b) BPR before ERP   c) WAP & its importance   d) Ubiquity, Content, Content Partnership15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) ERP and its Relation to E-Commerce

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) integrates all business functions — finance, manufacturing, HR, procurement, sales — in a single system with a shared database.

ERP–E-Commerce Relationship:

  • When an online order is placed, ERP automatically checks inventory, reserves stock, schedules fulfilment, and generates an invoice — all in real time.
  • ERP provides the "back-office" that powers the "front-office" e-commerce storefront.
  • Without ERP integration, an e-commerce site cannot accurately show real-time stock levels, calculate delivery dates, or process returns efficiently.
  • ERP feeds product data, pricing, and stock levels to the e-commerce platform; e-commerce feeds orders back to ERP.
  • Modern integration: SAP Commerce Cloud, Shopify + ERP connectors, Magento + SAP/Oracle.

b) BPR before ERP — Why Necessary?

See Q7(d) above for detailed reasoning. Summary: ERP systems embed best-practice processes. If existing processes are broken/inefficient, implementing ERP on top of them wastes investment. BPR first redesigns processes to be lean and optimal, then ERP automates those redesigned processes for maximum benefit.

c) WAP — Definition and Importance in E-Commerce

WAP (Wireless Application Protocol) was a technical standard that enabled mobile phones to access internet-based services (especially before smartphones). It defined how content should be formatted and transmitted to resource-constrained mobile devices over 2G/3G wireless networks.

  • WAP components: WAP Gateway (converts HTTP to WAP), WML (Wireless Markup Language — like HTML for mobile), WMLScript (like JavaScript for WAP).
  • Importance in early e-commerce: Allowed consumers to browse product catalogues, check order status, and make simple purchases from basic mobile phones — before smartphones existed.
  • Enabled m-commerce (mobile commerce) — banking alerts, ticket booking, stock trading on phones.
  • Largely superseded by modern smartphones with full browsers, HTML5, and mobile-optimised websites/apps — but WAP was the precursor to mobile e-commerce.

d) Ubiquity, Content, and Content Partnership

Ubiquity (in e-commerce): The ability to conduct commerce anywhere, anytime — not limited to a physical location or fixed computer. Mobile internet makes e-commerce ubiquitous: shop from a bus, pay from a café, track a parcel from anywhere.

Content (in e-commerce): Content refers to all digital information presented to users on an e-commerce platform — product descriptions, images, videos, reviews, blog posts, pricing, FAQs. Good content drives SEO, customer trust, and conversion.

Content Partnership: An agreement between two companies where one provides content (articles, product data, media) to appear on the other's platform — for mutual benefit. Examples:

  • A travel portal partnering with a hotel review site — hotel reviews appear on the travel portal.
  • An e-commerce site partnering with a tech blog — product reviews appear on the blog with purchase links.
  • News aggregators displaying publisher content in exchange for traffic referrals.
Q10a) Key wrapping   b) EDI elements + EDIFACT   c) ERP without e-commerce / e-commerce without ERP   d) Supply chain portal15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Key Wrapping

Key wrapping is a cryptographic technique where one encryption key is used to encrypt another key — "wrapping" it for secure storage or transmission. It is essential for key management in large systems.

  • How it works: A Key Encryption Key (KEK) (often an asymmetric RSA key or a strong symmetric key) encrypts a Content Encryption Key (CEK) (the actual data encryption key). The wrapped (encrypted) CEK is stored or transmitted safely.
  • Why useful: Protects session keys in transit; allows secure key distribution; enables key escrow (authorised recovery of keys); used in SET protocol and digital rights management (DRM).
  • Standards: AES Key Wrap (RFC 3394), PKCS#8 (private key wrapping).
Example:
  Session key (AES-128) used to encrypt EDI message
  ↓
  Session key itself encrypted using RSA public key
  ↓
  Encrypted session key sent with encrypted EDI message
  ↓
  Recipient decrypts session key with RSA private key → unwrapping

b) EDI Essential Elements + EDIFACT Standard

Essential elements of EDI:

  • Standard message format: Both parties must use same standard (X12, EDIFACT) — defines exact field positions and data types.
  • Translation software: Converts internal business data to/from EDI standard format.
  • Communication protocol: AS2 (HTTP-based, most common today), SFTP, VAN, or X.400.
  • Trading partner agreements: Legal/technical agreements on message types, acknowledgement (997/CONTRL), response times.
  • Acknowledgements: Functional Acknowledgement (FA) confirms message received and syntactically correct.

EDIFACT: See 2008-09 paper Q9(d) for full detail. International UN standard with hierarchical envelope structure (UNB/UNG/UNH), widely used in Europe and global trade.

c) ERP without e-Commerce / E-Commerce without ERP

(i) ERP without e-Commerce is not practical because:

  • ERP manages internal processes but cannot reach customers/suppliers digitally without e-commerce interfaces.
  • Customers expect 24×7 online ordering, self-service portals, and real-time availability — none possible without e-commerce layer.
  • B2B supply chain now requires electronic ordering (EDI/web portals) — ERP alone cannot support this without e-commerce integration.
  • Competitive market demands online presence — ERP-only companies lose customers to rivals with online channels.

(ii) E-Commerce without ERP is not practical because:

  • Online orders must trigger inventory, fulfilment, invoicing, accounting automatically — impossible without ERP integration.
  • Showing real-time stock levels, accurate delivery dates, and personalised pricing requires live ERP data.
  • Returns, refunds, and adjustments must flow back into accounting and inventory — manual without ERP.
  • At scale, manual back-office processing becomes a bottleneck — e-commerce collapses without ERP automation.

d) How a Supply Chain Portal Works

A supply chain portal is a web-based platform that serves as a central hub for all supply chain stakeholders — suppliers, manufacturers, distributors, logistics providers — to share information, collaborate, and transact.

Supplier A ──┐
Supplier B ──┤                    ┌── Manufacturer's ERP
Supplier C ──┼──→ [SC PORTAL] ←──┤── Warehouse system
3PL Carrier ─┤                    └── Customer orders
Retailer ────┘
  • Supplier self-service: Suppliers log in to view POs, confirm delivery dates, submit ASNs and invoices.
  • Demand visibility: Manufacturer shares rolling forecasts → suppliers can plan capacity.
  • Inventory visibility: All parties see current stock levels across the network.
  • Order tracking: Customers and distribution partners track shipment status in real time.
  • Exception management: Alerts for late deliveries, stock-outs, quality issues.
  • Document exchange: POs, invoices, and compliance documents managed electronically.
Q11Short notes on any three: a) Software Piracy   b) Call Centre   c) SCE Framework   d) Message Digest   e) Intrusion Detection System15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Software Piracy

Software piracy is the illegal copying, distribution, or use of software without authorisation from the copyright holder, in violation of the software's licence agreement.

  • Types: End-user piracy (installing on more machines than licensed), internet piracy (distributing crack files, keygen), counterfeiting (selling fake physical copies), hard-disk loading (OEMs pre-installing unlicensed software).
  • Impact on e-commerce: Pirated e-commerce software (shopping carts, payment systems) may lack security patches → vulnerabilities exploited by attackers.
  • Legal consequences: Civil suits, criminal prosecution, fines under copyright law (IT Act 2000 in India, DMCA in USA).
  • Prevention: Software licensing management, activation keys, cloud-based SaaS (eliminates local piracy), DRM.

b) Call Centre

A call centre is a centralised facility handling large volumes of inbound/outbound customer communications (phone, email, chat) for an organisation. See Q8(a) in the 2008-09 paper for full detail on equipment. Key additional points:

  • Modern call centres are omni-channel contact centres — handling phone, email, live chat, social media, and video.
  • KPIs: Average Handle Time (AHT), First Call Resolution (FCR), Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT), Net Promoter Score (NPS).
  • In e-commerce: essential for order queries, returns, payment disputes, and after-sales support.

c) SCE Framework

SCE (Supply Chain Execution) Framework is the operational layer of supply chain management responsible for the physical movement and tracking of goods. Core components:

  • WMS (Warehouse Management System): Receiving, put-away, picking, packing, shipping within warehouses.
  • TMS (Transportation Management System): Carrier selection, route optimisation, freight audit, track & trace.
  • OMS (Order Management System): Order capture, promising, fulfilment orchestration.
  • Manufacturing Execution System (MES): Shop floor execution — work orders, quality control.
  • Integration: SCE systems integrate with ERP (order data) and SCP (planning data) to bridge planning and execution.

d) Message Digest

A message digest (also called a cryptographic hash) is a fixed-length fingerprint of a message produced by a hash function. It uniquely represents the message content.

  • Properties: Deterministic (same input → same output), one-way (cannot reverse to get original), avalanche effect (tiny input change → completely different hash), collision-resistant (very hard to find two inputs with same hash).
  • Common algorithms: MD5 (128-bit), SHA-1 (160-bit), SHA-256 (256-bit — current standard).
  • Use in e-commerce: Verifying file/message integrity; digital signatures (hash encrypted = signature); password storage (store hash, not password); TLS certificate fingerprinting.

e) Intrusion Detection System (IDS)

An IDS is a security system that monitors network traffic and system activity for signs of malicious behaviour or policy violations and generates alerts.

  • Types: NIDS (Network-based — monitors all network traffic), HIDS (Host-based — monitors a single computer's logs and processes).
  • Detection methods: Signature-based (matches known attack patterns), Anomaly-based (flags deviations from normal behaviour), Stateful protocol analysis.
  • IDS vs IPS: IDS only detects and alerts; IPS (Intrusion Prevention System) actively blocks attacks.
  • e-Commerce use: Detects SQL injection attempts, brute-force login attacks, port scans, DDoS traffic spikes, data exfiltration.
  • Examples: Snort (open-source NIDS), OSSEC (HIDS), Suricata.
