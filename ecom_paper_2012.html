Q1(i)What combines purchase requests from multiple buyers into a single large order, which justifies a discount from the business?1M
a) Digital divide
b) Global digital divide
c) Demand aggregation
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (c) — Demand Aggregation

Demand aggregation (also called group buying or volume consolidation) pools purchase requests from multiple buyers to form one large combined order — meeting the supplier's minimum order quantity or qualifying for bulk discounts. Examples: GroupOn, buying cooperatives, procurement marketplaces that aggregate orders from multiple SMEs to negotiate enterprise pricing.

Q1(ii)Which segment do eBay, Amazon.com and LandsEnd.com belong?1M
a) B2Bs
b) B2Cs
c) C2Bs
d) C2Cs
✓ Answer: (b) — B2C

All three — eBay, Amazon.com, and LandsEnd.com — are primarily B2C (Business-to-Consumer) platforms that sell goods to individual consumers. While eBay also facilitates C2C sales and Amazon has a B2B marketplace, their primary and largest segment is B2C retail.

Q1(iii)Digital products are best suited for B2C e-commerce because they1M
a) Are commodity-like products
b) Can be mass-customised and personalised
c) Can be delivered at the time of purchase
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (d) — All of these

Digital products (software, ebooks, music, videos, online courses) are perfect for B2C e-commerce for all three reasons: they are commodity-like (standard versions sold to many), can be mass-customised (software can be personalised per user/licence), and can be delivered instantly at point of purchase — no shipping, no inventory holding, zero marginal cost of delivery.

Q1(iv)All of the following are techniques B2C e-commerce companies use to attract customers, EXCEPT1M
a) Registering with search engines
b) Viral marketing
c) Online ads
d) Virtual marketing
✓ Answer: (d) — Virtual marketing

Virtual marketing is not a standard recognised B2C customer acquisition technique. The real methods are: SEO/search engine registration (organic traffic), viral marketing (shareable content that spreads organically), and online ads (paid search, display, social media ads). "Virtual marketing" is a made-up distractor.

Q1(v)What is the percentage of customers who visit a Web site and actually buy something called?1M
a) Affiliate programs
b) Click-throughs
c) Spam
d) Conversion rate
✓ Answer: (d) — Conversion Rate

The conversion rate is the percentage of website visitors who complete a desired action (typically a purchase). Formula: Conversion Rate = (Number of purchases / Total visitors) × 100. Industry average for e-commerce is 1–4%. Improving conversion rate is a primary goal of e-commerce UX/CRO (Conversion Rate Optimisation) efforts.

Q1(vi)What are plastic cards of the size of a credit card that contains an embedded chip on which digital information can be stored?1M
a) CRM systems cards
b) E-government identity cards
c) FEDI cards
d) Smart cards
✓ Answer: (d) — Smart Cards

A smart card is a credit-card-sized plastic card with an embedded microprocessor chip that stores and processes digital data securely. Unlike magnetic stripe cards, smart cards can perform cryptographic operations. Uses: payment cards (EMV chip), national ID cards (Aadhaar, passport chips), transit cards, employee access badges, SIM cards in phones.

Q1(vii)Which of the following are advantages normally associated with B2B e-commerce?1M
a) Shorter cycle times
b) Reduction in costs
c) Reaches wider audiences
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (d) — All of these

B2B e-commerce provides: shorter cycle times (automated PO/invoice processing — days reduced to minutes), cost reduction (eliminate paper, postage, manual labour; better pricing through aggregation), and wider reach (global supplier/buyer discovery through B2B marketplaces like Alibaba, IndiaMart).

Q1(viii)If the suppliers in a particular industry hold a lot of power, the industry is1M
a) Saturated with alternative products or services
b) Using IT to gain a competitive advantage
c) More attractive to enter
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (d) — None of these

This is from Porter's Five Forces model. When suppliers hold high bargaining power, the industry is less attractive (not more), because suppliers can raise prices or reduce quality — squeezing buyer margins. None of the given options correctly describe the consequence. The correct consequence: industry participants face compressed margins and are at a disadvantage in negotiations with suppliers.

Q1(ix)Which statement describes switching costs?1M
a) There are many alternatives to using the product or service
b) There are few alternatives to using the product or service
c) High costs that make customers reluctant to switch to another product or service
d) Low costs that make customers want to switch to another product or service
✓ Answer: (c)

Switching costs are the costs (financial, time, effort, learning) a customer incurs when changing from one product/service/vendor to another. High switching costs lock customers in — they're reluctant to leave even if a better option exists. Examples: switching ERP vendors (massive re-implementation cost), changing mobile OS (losing app purchases, learning new UI), or switching cloud providers (data migration effort). High switching costs = competitive moat for the incumbent.

Q1(x)Which of the following is a useful tool when considering business strategy and IT?1M
a) Porter's Five Forces Model
b) Competitive Intelligence
c) General Business Model
d) B2B model
✓ Answer: (a) — Porter's Five Forces Model

Porter's Five Forces Model (Michael Porter, 1979) is a strategic framework for analysing industry competitiveness and determining how IT can create competitive advantage. The five forces: (1) Threat of new entrants, (2) Bargaining power of buyers, (3) Bargaining power of suppliers, (4) Threat of substitute products, (5) Rivalry among existing competitors. IT can reduce barriers to entry, increase switching costs, and enable differentiation — each mapped to these forces.

Q2Can ERP software be applied in i) Process Industry ii) Service Industry? Justify.5 Marks
✓ Answer

i) ERP in Process Industry

Yes — ERP can absolutely be applied in process industries (chemical, pharmaceutical, food & beverage, oil & gas, paint manufacturing) though with some specialisation needed.

  • Standard ERP modules used: Finance, HR, Procurement, Sales, Inventory, Quality Management.
  • Special requirements: Process industries have unique needs — batch management (track which batch of raw material went into which production run), recipe/formula management (BOM is a recipe, not a discrete parts list), by-product and co-product handling, shelf-life and expiry date tracking, compliance/regulatory documentation (FDA for pharma).
  • ERP solutions: SAP ERP for Process Industries (IS-PI), Oracle Process Manufacturing, Infor LN — all have process industry specific modules.
  • Justification: Process manufacturers have the same need as discrete manufacturers for integrated procurement, production, inventory, and finance management — ERP delivers this.

ii) ERP in Service Industry

Yes — ERP is widely used in service industries (consulting, banking, telecom, healthcare, education, hospitality) though the focus shifts from manufacturing modules to service-centric ones.

  • Modules used: Finance, HR/Payroll, CRM, Project Management, Billing, Asset Management.
  • Key difference: No physical inventory or manufacturing — "product" is a service delivered by people or systems.
  • Service ERP features: Professional Services Automation (PSA) — project costing, resource scheduling, timesheet management, client billing; Service Delivery Management for field service companies.
  • Examples: Hospitals use ERP for patient billing, pharmacy inventory, HR; banks use it for branch operations and HR; consulting firms for project tracking and billing.
  • Justification: Even without physical goods, service companies need integrated finance, HR, CRM, and project management — ERP provides exactly this.
Q3What are the risks for a small company connecting itself electronically with major customers?5 Marks
✓ Answer

When a small company connects electronically (EDI, B2B portal, extranet) with major customers like Walmart or Amazon, it gains business but faces significant risks:

  • High compliance cost: Major retailers mandate specific EDI standards, message types, timing, and error rates. Achieving and maintaining compliance requires significant technology investment — disproportionate for a small company.
  • Technology dependency: The small company becomes reliant on the customer's portal/system. If the customer changes its technology platform, the small company must adapt at its own cost.
  • Security vulnerabilities: Electronic connection expands the attack surface. A breach in the small company's system can expose the large customer's data — creating liability and potential business termination.
  • Power imbalance: Large customers dictate terms — payment terms, EDI specifications, labelling requirements, SLA penalties for non-compliance. Small companies have little negotiating leverage.
  • Single customer dependence: Electronic integration often deepens dependency on one major customer — if the customer switches suppliers, the small company's heavily customised systems become worthless.
  • Chargebacks and penalties: EDI errors (wrong shipment date, missing barcode, late ASN) trigger automatic chargebacks — significant financial penalties for small companies.
  • Business process disruption: Integration requires redesigning internal processes to meet customer requirements — disruptive and costly for a small operation.
Q4Briefly explain supply chain planning and a few software for SCP.5 Marks
✓ Answer

Supply Chain Planning (SCP) is the strategic and tactical process of aligning supply with anticipated demand across the supply chain — deciding what to produce, when, how much, and from which suppliers — before execution begins.

SCP Components

  • Demand Planning: Forecast future demand by SKU, region, and time period using statistical models, ML, and market intelligence.
  • Inventory Planning: Determine optimal stock levels — safety stock, reorder points, EOQ — at each node in the supply network.
  • Supply / Replenishment Planning: Translate demand forecasts into supply orders — material requirements, supplier sourcing, production scheduling.
  • S&OP (Sales & Operations Planning): Monthly cross-functional process aligning sales forecasts with production capacity and supply constraints.
  • Network Design: Strategic optimisation of warehouse locations, distribution lanes, and transportation modes.

SCP Software

  • SAP IBP (Integrated Business Planning): Cloud-based SCP suite — demand sensing, supply planning, S&OP, inventory optimisation.
  • Blue Yonder (JDA): AI-powered demand forecasting and supply planning — widely used in retail and consumer goods.
  • Oracle SCM Cloud: End-to-end SCP — demand management, supply chain collaboration, network design.
  • Kinaxis RapidResponse: Real-time concurrent supply chain planning — fast scenario modelling, widely used in high-tech and automotive.
  • Anaplan: Flexible planning platform — used for S&OP, demand planning, and financial planning.
Q5Define WAP. What are the handheld devices?5 Marks
✓ Answer

WAP — Wireless Application Protocol

WAP (Wireless Application Protocol) is a technical standard that enabled mobile phones and handheld devices to access internet-based services — specially formatted for the limited display, processing power, and bandwidth of early mobile devices operating on 2G/3G networks.

  • Key components: WAP Gateway (converts HTTP to WAP protocol), WML (Wireless Markup Language — stripped-down version of HTML), WMLScript (client-side scripting like JavaScript for WAP), WTLS (Wireless TLS — security layer).
  • WAP gateway: Translates between WAP devices (narrow-band, low-power) and standard web servers (HTTP/HTML).
  • Use in e-commerce: Enabled early mobile commerce — product browsing, order status, banking alerts, ticket booking — before smartphones existed.
  • Current status: WAP is largely obsolete — superseded by modern smartphones with full HTML5 browsers, 4G/5G, and mobile-responsive websites/apps.

Handheld Devices (used with WAP and mobile e-commerce)

  • Smartphones: Primary handheld today — full browsers, apps, cameras. (iPhone, Samsung Galaxy, etc.)
  • Feature phones / WAP phones: Earlier devices with basic WAP browsers (Nokia 3310 era).
  • PDAs (Personal Digital Assistants): Palm Pilot, HP iPAQ — early handheld computers with touchscreens and stylus.
  • Tablets: iPad, Samsung Galaxy Tab — larger touchscreen handhelds for productivity and browsing.
  • Pagers: Early one-way/two-way messaging devices — used for alerts.
  • Handheld barcode scanners: Used in warehouses and retail for inventory — connect to ERP via Wi-Fi.
  • Smartwatches: Wearable handhelds — notifications, payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay).
Q6What are the advantages and disadvantages of E-Cash?5 Marks
✓ Answer

Advantages of E-Cash

  • Anonymity: Like physical cash, properly implemented e-cash (using blind signatures) cannot be traced back to the buyer — privacy in transactions.
  • Convenience: Payments made instantly from any device — no physical wallet, no ATM visit.
  • Low transaction fees: Especially for micropayments — much cheaper than credit card processing fees (which are uneconomical below ~₹20).
  • No credit required: Unlike credit cards, no credit check or bank account needed — accessible to the unbanked.
  • Global transactions: Cross-border payments without foreign exchange conversion delays and fees.
  • Offline capable: Stored-value e-cash can work without internet for some implementations.

Disadvantages of E-Cash

  • Security risk: If e-wallet file is deleted, corrupted, or stolen, the money is gone — unlike physical cash, no central authority can reverse it.
  • Double-spending risk: Without proper bank tracking, the same digital coin could theoretically be spent twice — requires robust bank verification systems.
  • No universal acceptance: E-cash is not accepted everywhere — depends on merchant adoption.
  • Technology dependency: Requires device, internet, and compatible software — not accessible to all.
  • Anonymity misuse: The same anonymity that protects privacy can enable money laundering, tax evasion, and illegal transactions.
  • Limited regulatory framework: E-cash systems are less regulated than banking — consumer protection is weaker.
  • Inflation of digital currency: Poorly designed systems risk unlimited issuance of e-coins — needs strict banking controls.

Q7a) Steps for implementing IT software for SCM   b) Do steps vary by package? How standardised?15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Steps for Implementing IT/SCM Software Packages

  • Step 1 — Business case & needs assessment: Define SCM pain points, desired outcomes, ROI justification. Identify gaps in current systems.
  • Step 2 — Package selection: RFP/RFI process — evaluate vendors (SAP SCM, Oracle, Blue Yonder) on functionality, TCO, integration capability, vendor stability, references.
  • Step 3 — Business process mapping (BPR): Map current ("as-is") processes. Redesign to-be processes aligned with the package's built-in best practices. Critical to do this before configuration.
  • Step 4 — System design & configuration: Configure the package to the redesigned processes — organisational structure, master data (vendors, products, warehouses), planning parameters, workflow rules.
  • Step 5 — Integration: Connect SCM software with ERP, WMS, TMS, EDI, and customer/supplier portals via APIs or middleware. Test all interfaces.
  • Step 6 — Data migration: Cleanse and migrate historical data (supplier master, product master, open orders, inventory balances) from legacy systems.
  • Step 7 — Testing: Unit testing → integration testing → UAT (User Acceptance Testing) with real business scenarios. Performance testing under peak load.
  • Step 8 — Training: Train end users, planners, and system administrators on new workflows and system navigation.
  • Step 9 — Go-live & cutover: Phased rollout (by business unit or geography) or big-bang cutover. Maintain parallel run if risk is high.
  • Step 10 — Post go-live support & optimisation: Hypercare period (intensive support), stabilisation, and then continuous improvement — tune planning parameters, retrain models.

b) Do Steps Vary Package to Package? How Are They Standardised?

Yes, steps vary somewhat by package — but the overall framework is largely standardised through industry methodologies:

  • Package-specific variations: SAP uses ASAP (Accelerated SAP) / now SAP Activate methodology with specific "fit-to-standard" workshops. Oracle uses Oracle Unified Method (OUM). Each vendor provides their own implementation toolkit, templates, and accelerators.
  • Standardisation through frameworks:
    • PMBOK (Project Management Body of Knowledge): Project governance, risk management, scope definition.
    • SCOR (Supply Chain Operations Reference) Model: Standardises SC processes into Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Return — provides a common language for SCM implementation regardless of software.
    • APICS/ASCM standards: Supply chain professional certifications (CPIM, CSCP) define standard SC processes.
    • System integrator methodologies: Large SIs (Accenture, Deloitte, IBM) have proprietary implementation methodologies standardised across all their projects.
  • Common elements regardless of package: BPR, data migration, integration, testing, training, and change management are universal — every implementation needs them.
Q8a) Benefits of EDI   b) EDI service providers in India   c) Extranet vs Intranet15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Potential Benefits of Using EDI

  • Speed: Business documents exchanged in minutes vs days for paper — dramatically accelerates the order-to-cash and procure-to-pay cycles.
  • Accuracy: No manual re-keying → data entry error rate drops to near zero → fewer disputes, fewer costly corrections.
  • Cost reduction: Eliminates paper, printing, postage, fax, filing, and manual processing labour. Studies show cost per transaction drops from $30–50 (paper) to under $1 (EDI).
  • Process automation: Receipt of an EDI PO automatically triggers inventory check, order acknowledgement, production scheduling, and shipment planning — no human action required.
  • Improved inventory management: Real-time demand signals and ASNs enable JIT inventory — reduces safety stock and carrying costs.
  • Better supplier/customer relationships: Reliable, fast, accurate transactions build trust; fewer disputes improve partnership quality.
  • Compliance: Major retailers (Walmart, Target, Amazon) mandate EDI — compliance is a condition of doing business.
  • Audit trail: Complete electronic record of every transaction — simplifies audits, dispute resolution, and regulatory compliance.
  • Environmental benefit: Dramatically reduces paper consumption.

b) EDI Service Providers in India

  • VSNL (now Tata Communications): One of the earliest EDI VANs in India — provided EDI services through its INDONET network. Offered X.400 and internet-based EDI.
  • CMC Limited (now TCS): Provided EDI services and translation software for Indian businesses.
  • NICSI / NIC (National Informatics Centre): Government-mandated EDI for customs (ICEGATE portal) — all import/export documentation filed electronically.
  • ICEGATE (Indian Customs EDI Gateway): Central customs EDI system — Bill of Entry, Shipping Bill, manifest filing all done via EDI with customs.
  • Sify Technologies: Provided EDI VAN services for Indian corporate customers.
  • Sterling Commerce / IBM Sterling: International EDI provider with India presence — AS2-based EDI for multinational supply chains.

General terms and conditions of EDI service providers: Monthly subscription/per-transaction fees; guaranteed SLA uptime (99.9%); support for specific standards (X12, EDIFACT); mailbox storage charges; trading partner management; connectivity via VAN, AS2, or SFTP; message validation and error reporting services.

Q9a) Main disadvantages of E-Commerce   b) The Four C's of e-commerce15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Main Disadvantages of E-Commerce

  • Security and fraud: Payment fraud, identity theft, phishing, data breaches — customers fear their financial information being stolen online.
  • Lack of personal touch: Cannot replicate in-store experience — no ability to physically examine, smell, or try products. High returns result.
  • Delivery dependency: Physical goods require logistics — prone to delays, damage, wrong deliveries; cannot match instant in-store fulfilment.
  • Digital divide: Excludes elderly, rural, and low-income populations without internet access, devices, or digital literacy.
  • Technology failure: Server downtime, website bugs, payment gateway failures mean lost sales and poor customer experience.
  • Overwhelming choice: Too many options cause decision fatigue — "paradox of choice" reduces conversion rates.
  • SEO and visibility challenges: Millions of e-commerce sites compete for search rankings — expensive and technically demanding.
  • Legal/regulatory complexity: Cross-border e-commerce faces customs, VAT/GST, consumer protection laws across multiple jurisdictions.
  • High return rates: Online purchases have 3× higher return rates vs in-store — reverse logistics is costly.
  • Customer trust: Buyers must trust a seller they may never meet — building trust online is harder than face-to-face.

b) The Four C's of e-Commerce

The Four C's represent the key pillars of e-commerce strategy:

              ┌────────────────────────────────────┐
              │         E-COMMERCE STRATEGY         │
              │                                      │
              │   CONVERGENCE   │   COLLABORATION   │
              │─────────────────┼───────────────────│
              │     CONTENT     │    COMMUNITY      │
              └────────────────────────────────────┘
  • 1. Convergence: The merging of different technologies (telecom, computing, media) into integrated digital platforms. A single device (smartphone) now serves as phone, computer, camera, TV, bank, and shop. For e-commerce, convergence means one app handles browsing, payment, delivery tracking, and customer service.
  • 2. Collaboration: Working with technology partners, suppliers, logistics providers, and even competitors to create better value for customers. Includes EDI integration with suppliers, API connections with payment gateways, 3PL partnerships for delivery. Collaborative Computing enables joint product development (CPD) and CPFR.
  • 3. Content: All information presented to customers — product descriptions, images, videos, reviews, blogs, FAQs. Quality content drives SEO (organic traffic), builds trust, and converts browsers to buyers. Content strategy is central to e-commerce success — rich product content reduces returns; educational content builds brand authority.
  • 4. Community: Building a network of engaged users around the brand or platform. Community features: product reviews, user forums, Q&A sections, loyalty programmes, social commerce. A strong community creates network effects — more users attract more users (eBay, Amazon Marketplace sellers). Community feedback also drives product improvement.
Q10a) Define ERP, tangible & intangible benefits   b) Why companies pursue new ERP   c) Functional & technical modules15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) ERP — Tangible and Intangible Benefits

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) integrates all core business functions into one system with a shared database. See earlier papers for full definition.

Tangible (quantifiable) benefits:

  • Inventory reduction (typically 15–35%)
  • Reduction in IT operating costs (fewer disparate systems to maintain)
  • Reduction in headcount for administrative/data-entry roles
  • Faster financial close (from 10 days to 3 days)
  • Reduced procurement costs through consolidated purchasing (5–15% savings)
  • Reduction in order fulfilment time
  • Reduced overtime due to automation
  • Lower carrying costs from improved inventory turns

Intangible (non-quantifiable) benefits:

  • Improved decision-making quality through real-time information
  • Standardised processes across all locations
  • Better customer service and satisfaction
  • Improved employee morale (less frustrating manual work)
  • Enhanced visibility and transparency across the organisation
  • Competitive positioning — "table stakes" for enterprise credibility
  • Improved regulatory compliance posture
  • Better collaboration across departments (shared data, common language)

b) Why Companies Pursue a New ERP Solution

  • Legacy system end-of-life: Old ERP vendor ending support — security risk and no new features.
  • Business growth: Existing system cannot handle increased transaction volumes, new geographies, or additional business units.
  • Merger/acquisition: Need to consolidate two separate ERP systems into one unified platform.
  • Digital transformation: Move to cloud ERP (SaaS) to reduce infrastructure cost and access latest capabilities.
  • Better analytics: Need for real-time dashboards and AI-powered insights the current system can't provide.
  • Regulatory changes: New compliance requirements (GST, GDPR, new accounting standards) the old system can't support.
  • Process improvement: Current system doesn't support desired processes — new ERP allows process redesign.
  • Integration needs: Current system can't easily integrate with e-commerce, CRM, or modern supply chain tools.

c) Functional and Technical Modules in ERP

Functional Modules (business process modules):

  • FI — Financial Accounting: General ledger, accounts payable, accounts receivable, asset accounting, financial statements.
  • CO — Controlling: Cost centre accounting, profit centre accounting, product costing, profitability analysis.
  • MM — Materials Management: Procurement, inventory management, goods receipt, invoice verification.
  • SD — Sales & Distribution: Sales orders, pricing, delivery, billing, customer management.
  • PP — Production Planning: MRP, capacity planning, production orders, shop floor control.
  • HR/HCM — Human Resources: Payroll, time management, recruitment, organisational management.
  • QM — Quality Management: Quality inspection, defect recording, certificates.
  • PM — Plant Maintenance: Equipment maintenance, work orders, preventive maintenance.
  • PS — Project System: Project planning, budgeting, milestone tracking.

Technical Modules / Infrastructure:

  • BC — Basis (SAP): System administration, user management, transport management, performance tuning.
  • ABAP Workbench: ERP's proprietary programming environment for custom development and reports.
  • Workflow: Automated business process routing — approval workflows, notifications.
  • ALE/IDOC: Application Link Enabling — inter-system communication; IDOC is the EDI wrapper format.
  • Security and Authorisation: Role-based access control — who can view/edit which transactions.
  • Data Dictionary: Metadata repository — defines all data structures and relationships.
Q11Short notes on any three: a) B2B   b) Data Warehouse   c) SCE Framework   d) Data Encryption Methodology   e) Decision Support System15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) B2B — Business-to-Business e-Commerce

B2B e-commerce involves electronic transactions between two businesses — e.g., a manufacturer buying raw materials from a supplier, or a wholesaler selling to retailers.

  • Scale: B2B e-commerce is 5–6× larger than B2C globally — trillions of dollars annually.
  • Characteristics: Larger order values, longer sales cycles, negotiated pricing, credit terms, complex approval workflows.
  • Models: Supplier-centric (Cisco.com), buyer-centric (Walmart supplier portal), neutral marketplace (Alibaba, IndiaMart, Udaan).
  • Technologies: EDI, B2B portals, punchout catalogues (OCI/cXML), API integrations, RFQ platforms.
  • Key advantage over traditional B2B: 24×7 self-service ordering, real-time price/availability, automated order processing, complete purchase history.

b) Data Warehouse

See 2010-11 Q11(d) for full detail. Summary: A large, centralised repository of integrated, historical, cleaned data from multiple operational systems — optimised for analytical queries (OLAP) rather than transactions (OLTP). Key components: ETL pipeline, star/snowflake schema, OLAP cubes, BI tools. In e-commerce: consolidates data from web, stores, app, EDI — enables management reporting, customer analytics, and supply chain KPIs. Examples: Amazon Redshift, Google BigQuery, Snowflake.

c) SCE Framework

See 2008-09 Q11(b) and 2010-11 Q11(e) for full detail. The Supply Chain Execution framework manages the physical movement of goods through WMS (warehouse), TMS (transport), OMS (orders), and MES (manufacturing floor) — bridging supply chain planning with real-world execution. Key metrics: order fill rate, on-time delivery, warehouse throughput, freight cost per unit.

d) Data Encryption Methodology

Data encryption transforms readable plaintext into unreadable ciphertext using an algorithm and key — only authorised parties with the correct key can reverse it.

  • Symmetric encryption: Same key encrypts and decrypts. Fast for bulk data. Examples: AES-256 (current standard), DES (obsolete), 3DES. Used for: database encryption, file encryption, VPN bulk data.
  • Asymmetric encryption: Public key encrypts, private key decrypts (or vice versa for signatures). Slower but solves key distribution. Examples: RSA, ECC, ElGamal. Used for: key exchange, digital signatures, certificates.
  • Hybrid encryption: Asymmetric to exchange a symmetric session key; symmetric for bulk data. Used in: TLS/HTTPS, PGP, SET. Best of both worlds.
  • Hashing (one-way): SHA-256, SHA-3, MD5 — fixed-length fingerprint, cannot be reversed. Used for: password storage, file integrity, digital signatures.
  • In e-commerce: TLS 1.3 encrypts all HTTPS traffic; AES-256 encrypts stored payment data; RSA/ECC for certificate key exchange; SHA-256 for signed receipts and certificates.

e) Decision Support System (DSS)

A DSS (Decision Support System) is an information system that supports business decision-making by collecting, analysing, and presenting data in a way that helps managers make better choices — especially for semi-structured and unstructured problems.

  • Components: Data management (database + data warehouse), Model management (statistical/optimisation models), User interface (dashboards, reports, what-if tools).
  • Types: Model-driven DSS (simulation, optimisation), Data-driven DSS (data mining, OLAP), Knowledge-driven DSS (expert systems).
  • In e-commerce: Pricing optimisation DSS (dynamic pricing), demand forecasting DSS, inventory replenishment DSS, fraud detection system, customer churn prediction.
  • vs MIS: MIS provides routine reports on past performance; DSS supports ad-hoc analysis and future decision-making.
  • Examples: Tableau (data-driven DSS), SAP Analytics Cloud, Power BI, SAS analytics platform.
