Q1(i)Symmetric encryption involves1M
a) Public key
b) Private key
c) Both of these
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (b)

Symmetric encryption uses the same single secret (private) key for both encryption and decryption. Both parties must share this key securely beforehand. Examples: DES, AES, 3DES, RC4. It does NOT use a public key — that is asymmetric encryption.

Q1(iii)All e-commerce applications follow the ________ model.1M
a) Client-server
b) Distributed
c) Mainframe
d) None of these
✓ Answer: (a) — Client-server

All e-commerce applications fundamentally follow the Client-Server model: the user's browser (client) sends HTTP/HTTPS requests to a web server (server) which processes them and returns responses. The client displays the UI; the server handles business logic, database access, and data processing. This applies whether the app is a B2C store, B2B portal, or payment gateway.

PGP (Pretty Good Privacy), PEM (Privacy Enhanced Mail), and S/MIME (Secure/Multipurpose Internet Mail Extensions) are all secure messaging / email security protocols. PGP uses web-of-trust; PEM was an early IETF standard; S/MIME is the modern standard built into most email clients (Outlook, Apple Mail).

Q1(vii)In RSA algorithm ________ numbers are involved.1M

b) EVEN
c) PRIME
d) All of these
✓ Answer: (c) — PRIME

RSA's security is based on choosing two large prime numbers P and Q, computing N = P×Q, and exploiting the mathematical difficulty of factoring N back into P and Q. The strength of RSA increases with the size of the primes — modern RSA uses 2048-bit or 4096-bit key lengths, requiring primes of 1024+ bits.

Q2What is e-Governance & how does it work?5 Marks
✓ Answer

e-Governance is the use of ICT (Information and Communication Technology) by government to deliver services to citizens (G2C), businesses (G2B), government agencies (G2G), and employees (G2E) — improving efficiency, transparency, accessibility, and accountability.

How e-Governance Works

  • Front-end portals: Government builds secure web portals (or mobile apps) where citizens/businesses access services — e.g., income-tax filing portal, passport Seva, DigiLocker.
  • Identity authentication: Citizens authenticate using national ID (Aadhaar, PAN), OTP, or digital certificate to access personalised services securely.
  • Digital forms and workflows: Paper forms replaced by online forms — submitted data flows electronically through approval workflows within government departments.
  • Backend system integration: Front-end portal connects to back-end databases (citizen registry, land records, tax systems) via secure APIs — real-time data retrieval and updates.
  • Electronic payments: Fees, taxes, and fines paid online via net banking, UPI, or cards — receipts generated automatically.
  • Document management: Digital document storage and retrieval (DigiLocker) — no physical paper filing required.
  • Transparency mechanisms: RTI portals, tender/e-procurement platforms (GeM), public grievance portals — accountability to citizens.

Examples: IRCTC (railway bookings), Income Tax e-filing, GSTN (GST registration and filing), e-Courts, UMANG App (1200+ services).

Q5What are the advantages & disadvantages of e-Commerce over normal commerce?5 Marks
✓ Answer

Advantages of e-Commerce over Traditional Commerce

  • 24×7 operation: No store hours — customers shop any time, from anywhere.
  • Global market: A small business can sell worldwide without physical expansion.
  • Lower operational cost: No retail rent, fewer staff, no physical inventory display costs.
  • Wider product selection: No shelf-space limit — offer thousands of SKUs online.
  • Price transparency: Easy comparison shopping drives competitive pricing — benefits consumers.
  • Personalisation: Recommendation engines, targeted promotions based on customer data.
  • Instant digital delivery: Software, music, ebooks delivered immediately upon purchase.
  • Customer data: Rich behavioural and purchase analytics enable better business decisions.

Disadvantages of e-Commerce over Traditional Commerce

  • No sensory experience: Cannot touch, smell, try on, or taste products — higher return rates (especially fashion, food).
  • Security and fraud risks: Payment fraud, phishing, data breaches — consumer trust issues.
  • Delivery wait: Physical goods require shipping time — cannot match instant in-store fulfilment.
  • Digital divide: Excludes those without internet access, devices, or digital skills.
  • Technology dependence: Server downtime = lost business; requires continuous IT maintenance.
  • Lack of personal service: No face-to-face interaction — harder to build personal relationships for complex sales.
  • Return logistics: Returns are more complex and costly than in-store returns.
Q6What is Firewall? State the functions of firewall in e-Commerce.5 Marks
✓ Answer

A Firewall is a network security system (hardware, software, or both) that monitors and controls network traffic between a trusted internal network and untrusted external networks based on predefined security rules. It is the first line of defence for an e-commerce infrastructure.

Functions in e-Commerce

  • Traffic filtering: Allows only legitimate traffic (HTTPS port 443, HTTP port 80) to the web server; blocks all other inbound ports.
  • DMZ protection: Places web server in a DMZ, isolating it from the internal payment and database servers.
  • IP blacklisting: Blocks known malicious IP addresses, bot networks, and suspicious geographic regions.
  • DoS/DDoS mitigation: Rate-limits connections; detects and blocks flood attacks before they overwhelm the web server.
  • Application inspection: Proxy/WAF (Web Application Firewall) inspects HTTP content for SQL injection, XSS, and other web attacks.
  • VPN support: Terminates VPN connections for secure remote admin and B2B partner access.
  • Audit logging: Logs all traffic for security incident investigation and PCI-DSS compliance reporting.

Q7a) What is ERP?   b) BPR — what & why before ERP   c) ERP & e-commerce relation   d) Direct benefits of ERP15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) ERP — Enterprise Resource Planning

ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is an integrated software system that automates and manages all core business functions — Finance, HR, Manufacturing, Supply Chain, Procurement, Sales, and Distribution — through a single unified database shared across the entire organisation in real time.

Key characteristics: Single database (no data silos), modular architecture (implement modules as needed), real-time data, process automation, best-practice workflows built in.

Leading ERP vendors: SAP (S/4HANA), Oracle (ERP Cloud / NetSuite), Microsoft (Dynamics 365), Infor, Epicor.

b) Business Process Redesign (BPR) — What it is and Why it must precede ERP

BPR is the fundamental rethinking and radical redesign of business processes to achieve dramatic improvements in cost, quality, speed, and service. It discards existing processes and redesigns from scratch, enabled by IT.

Why BPR before ERP is essential:

  • ERP contains best practices: Implementing ERP on inefficient old processes locks in those inefficiencies at scale — "automating a mess".
  • Reduces customisation: When redesigned processes match ERP's built-in flows, expensive and risky customisation is minimised.
  • Maximises ROI: BPR + ERP together yield 10× improvement; ERP alone on broken processes yields marginal gain at high cost.
  • Change readiness: BPR creates organisational buy-in for change before ERP disrupts daily operations.
  • Lower failure risk: Most ERP failures occur because processes weren't redesigned — staff revert to old ways when new system doesn't fit their workflows.

c) Relationship between ERP and e-Commerce

  • ERP is the backbone: e-Commerce storefront is the customer-facing front-end; ERP is the back-office engine that fulfils what was ordered online.
  • Real-time inventory: e-Commerce site shows accurate stock levels only because it queries ERP inventory in real time.
  • Order-to-cash automation: Online order → ERP picks, packs, ships, invoices, and updates accounts automatically.
  • Product data: ERP master data (product descriptions, prices, weights) feeds the e-commerce catalogue.
  • Customer data: CRM module in ERP provides order history and preferences for personalisation on the e-commerce site.
  • Without integration: e-Commerce orders must be manually re-entered into ERP — error-prone, slow, and unscalable.

d) Direct Benefits of ERP

  • Inventory reduction: Accurate real-time inventory data eliminates over-ordering and safety stock excess — typical 20–30% inventory reduction.
  • Faster order fulfilment: Automated order-to-ship workflow — days reduced to hours.
  • Reduced headcount: Automation of data-entry tasks — fewer staff needed for routine transaction processing.
  • Lower IT costs: One integrated system replaces many expensive, poorly integrated legacy systems.
  • Faster financial close: Period-end close takes days instead of weeks — real-time financial reporting.
  • Better procurement: Automated PO generation, vendor performance tracking, and consolidated purchasing — reduces purchase prices 5–15%.
  • Improved on-time delivery: Production planning integration ensures materials available when production starts — fewer delays.
  • Error reduction: Single data entry eliminates re-keying errors across systems.
Q8a) What is EDI?   b) Basic components   c) Benefits over non-EDI   d) Importance of EDI in e-commerce15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) EDI — Electronic Data Interchange

EDI is the direct computer-to-computer exchange of standardised business documents (purchase orders, invoices, shipping notices, payment instructions) between trading partners in a machine-readable format — replacing paper-based processes completely. No human intervention is needed in the transaction flow.

b) Basic Components of EDI

  • EDI Standards: Define the exact document structure — ANSI X12 (North America), UN/EDIFACT (international), HL7 (healthcare). Both parties must use the same standard.
  • Translation Software: Converts company's internal data (ERP flat file, database records) to the standard EDI format for sending, and converts received EDI back to internal format.
  • Communication Network: The transmission medium — VAN (Value Added Network), Internet/AS2 protocol, SFTP, or AS4.
  • VAN (Value Added Network): Third-party service providing secure, store-and-forward "mailbox" service — buffers messages until recipient is ready to receive.
  • Trading Partner Agreements: Define which message types will be exchanged, frequency, acknowledgement requirements, and data element usage.
  • Functional Acknowledgement: Automated receipt confirmation (997 in X12 / CONTRL in EDIFACT) confirms message was received and syntactically valid.

c) Benefits of EDI over Non-EDI

AspectEDI SystemNon-EDI (Paper/Manual)
SpeedDocuments transmitted and processed in minutesDays to weeks via post/fax/manual email
AccuracySystem-to-system — no manual re-keying errorsManual data entry causes 1–5% error rates
CostNear-zero marginal cost per transactionPaper, postage, labour, filing — $30–50 per paper PO
Process automationPO receipt triggers automatic inventory check, acknowledgement, production planningEach step requires human action
InventoryJIT-capable — precise, timely orders reduce safety stockLonger order cycles require larger safety stock buffers
Audit trailComplete electronic log of every transactionPaper files — difficult to search, easy to lose
Partner requirementsRequired by Walmart, Tesco, Amazon for all suppliersCannot do business with major retailers without EDI

d) Importance of EDI in e-Commerce

  • B2B order processing: EDI is the backbone of B2B e-commerce — POs, order acknowledgements, and invoices flow automatically between ERP systems of buyer and seller.
  • Supplier integration: e-Commerce businesses need reliable supplier connections — EDI provides real-time, automated procurement.
  • Inventory accuracy: EDI ASNs (Advance Ship Notices) update inventory systems before goods arrive — enables accurate "in-stock" signals on e-commerce sites.
  • Marketplace compliance: Selling on Amazon, Walmart Marketplace, or major retailers requires EDI compliance — mandatory for vendor certification.
  • Financial settlement: EDI REMADV (Remittance Advice) automates payment notification and accounts receivable matching.
  • Scale: An e-commerce business processing thousands of orders per day cannot manually manage supplier transactions — EDI enables the volume.
Q9a) Supply chain of e-commerce   b) Why SCM is vital   c) e-SC planning tools   d) SCM portals15 Marks

a) Supply Chain of e-Commerce

The end-to-end network moving products from raw material suppliers through manufacturers, distributors, and fulfilment centres to the end customer's doorstep — triggered by an online order. Unique e-commerce SC features: high volume small orders, last-mile complexity, high return rates, demand volatility.

b) Why SCM is Vital

Delivery IS the customer experience in e-commerce. Speed (Amazon Prime effect), inventory accuracy (no stockouts online), cost (logistics = 10–30% of revenue), scalability (handling demand spikes), and return management are all supply chain functions that directly determine e-commerce success or failure.

c) e-Supply Chain Planning Tools

Demand forecasting (Blue Yonder, SAP IBP), inventory optimisation, supply planning, S&OP, network design, and CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting & Replenishment with suppliers). These tools bridge planning with execution — preventing stockouts and over-stocking.

d) SCM Portals

Web platforms connecting all SC stakeholders — supplier PO/ASN portal, real-time inventory visibility, customer order tracking portal, exception management dashboards, 3PL integration, and analytics. Replaces phone/email chasing with self-service data access for all parties.

Q10a) Role of cryptography in e-commerce   b) RSA & DES algorithms   c) Threats of e-commerce15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Role of Cryptography in e-Commerce

Cryptography is the science of securing information by transforming it into an unreadable form (ciphertext) that can only be reversed by authorised parties. In e-commerce it provides:

  • Confidentiality: Payment card numbers, personal data, and business transactions are encrypted in transit (TLS/HTTPS) — cannot be read if intercepted.
  • Authentication: Digital certificates verify that you're talking to the genuine website (amazon.com), not a phishing clone.
  • Integrity: Hash functions (SHA-256) detect any tampering with transmitted data — ensures orders and payments arrive unaltered.
  • Non-repudiation: Digital signatures provide legal proof of transactions — seller cannot deny a completed sale, buyer cannot deny a payment.
  • Secure payments: SET protocol, SSL/TLS, tokenisation of card data — all built on cryptographic primitives.
  • Password security: User passwords stored as cryptographic hashes (bcrypt, Argon2) — not in plaintext.
  • PCI-DSS compliance: Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard mandates encryption of cardholder data at rest and in transit.

b) RSA Algorithm

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman, 1977) is the most widely used asymmetric encryption algorithm, based on the mathematical difficulty of factoring the product of two large prime numbers.

Key Generation:
  1. Choose two large primes: P and Q
  2. Compute: N = P × Q
  3. Compute: φ(N) = (P−1)(Q−1)
  4. Choose E: 1 < E < φ(N), gcd(E, φ(N)) = 1
  5. Find D: E × D ≡ 1 mod φ(N)  [Extended Euclidean]
  Public key: (E, N)   Private key: (D, N)

Encryption:   C = M^E mod N
Decryption:   M = C^D mod N

Example (P=17, Q=29):
  N=493, φ(N)=448, E=3, D=299
  Encrypt M=10: C = 10^3 mod 493 = 1000 mod 493 = 507... 
  Decrypt:      M = C^299 mod 493 = 10

Uses in e-commerce: HTTPS key exchange, digital signatures, SET protocol, email encryption (PGP/S/MIME).

DES Algorithm

DES (Data Encryption Standard, 1977) is a symmetric block cipher developed by IBM and adopted as a US federal standard.

Key features:
  • Block size: 64 bits
  • Key size: 56 bits (effective — 8 bits are parity)
  • Rounds: 16 rounds of Feistel network
  • Structure: Initial permutation → 16 rounds of
    subkey mixing + S-box substitution + permutation
    → Final permutation

Process:
  64-bit plaintext block
      ↓ Initial Permutation (IP)
      ↓ 16 rounds:
        - Split into L (left 32 bits) and R (right 32 bits)
        - L(i) = R(i−1)
        - R(i) = L(i−1) XOR f(R(i−1), K(i))
        - f uses expansion, XOR with subkey, S-box, P-box
      ↓ Final Permutation (IP⁻¹)
  64-bit ciphertext block

Weakness: 56-bit key is too short — broken by brute force in 1998. Replaced by 3DES (triple DES) and ultimately AES (Advanced Encryption Standard).

c) Threats of e-Commerce

  • Phishing: Fake emails/sites mimic trusted brands to steal credentials and payment info.
  • Payment fraud: Stolen card details used for CNP (Card Not Present) fraud — very common in online transactions.
  • SQL Injection: Malicious SQL in form fields accesses/modifies the database — steals customer records.
  • Cross-Site Scripting (XSS): Injected scripts run in other users' browsers — steals session cookies.
  • DDoS Attacks: Floods server with traffic — site goes offline, revenue lost.
  • Man-in-the-Middle (MITM): Attacker intercepts and alters communication between browser and server.
  • Data breaches: Customer PII and payment data stolen from databases (e.g., Magecart attacks on e-commerce checkout pages).
  • Ransomware: Encrypts e-commerce infrastructure — operations halt until ransom paid.
  • Account takeover: Credential stuffing attacks use leaked passwords to hijack customer accounts.
Q11Short notes on any three: a) Call Centre   b) EDIFACT   c) Software Piracy   d) Collaborative Computing   e) Digital Signature15 Marks
✓ Answer

a) Call Centre

A call centre is a centralised facility handling inbound/outbound customer communications. Key equipment: ACD, IVR, CTI, agent workstations, headsets, PBX, LAN, call recording. Modes: inbound, outbound, blended, virtual. KPIs: AHT, FCR, CSAT. Modern evolution → omni-channel contact centre (phone, email, chat, social). Full detail in 2008-09 Q8.

b) EDIFACT

UN/EDIFACT (Electronic Data Interchange for Administration, Commerce and Transport) is the international EDI standard developed by the United Nations. Used globally — especially in Europe, Asia, and international trade.

  • Structure: Interchange (UNB/UNZ) → Functional Group (UNG/UNE) → Message (UNH/UNT) → Segments → Data Elements.
  • Key messages: ORDERS, ORDRSP, DESADV, INVOIC, REMADV, CONTRL, DELFOR.
  • Delimiter syntax: Uses + (element separator), : (component separator), ' (segment terminator).
  • vs X12: EDIFACT = international; ANSI X12 = North American domestic standard.
  • Maintained by UNECE. Widely used in automotive (Odette), retail (GS1), healthcare.

c) Software Piracy

Software piracy is the illegal copying, distribution, or use of copyrighted software without a valid licence. Full detail in 2009-10 Q11(a). Additional context: India's IT Act 2000 and Copyright Act 1957 penalise software piracy with imprisonment up to 3 years and fines. BSA (Business Software Alliance) actively pursues piracy globally. Cloud/SaaS delivery has dramatically reduced piracy by removing local software installation entirely.

d) Collaborative Computing

Collaborative Computing refers to using technology to enable multiple people or systems in different locations to work together on shared tasks, documents, or processes in real time or asynchronously.

  • Tools: Video conferencing (Zoom, Teams), shared document editing (Google Docs, Office 365), project management (Jira, Asana), wikis, shared whiteboards (Miro).
  • In e-commerce: Cross-functional teams (marketing, logistics, IT) collaborate on promotions; suppliers and buyers co-design products (CPD); customer service agents share knowledge bases.
  • CSCW (Computer-Supported Cooperative Work): Academic field studying collaborative computing systems.
  • Benefits: Faster decision-making, reduced travel, access to global expertise, 24×7 collaboration across time zones.
  • In SCM: CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment) uses collaborative computing to share demand forecasts between retailers and suppliers.

e) Digital Signature

A digital signature is a cryptographic mechanism that provides authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation for digital messages and documents. Full detail in 2008-09 Q7(a).

How it works:
  Sender:   Hash(Message) → encrypt with PRIVATE key → Signature
  Receiver: Decrypt signature with PUBLIC key → H'
            Hash received message → H
            If H == H' → signature valid ✓

Legal status: Digital signatures are legally binding in India
under the IT Act 2000 and internationally under UNCITRAL
Model Law on Electronic Signatures.

Applications: E-mail signing (S/MIME, PGP), document signing (PDF, contracts), software distribution (code signing), e-governance (Aadhaar e-KYC), SET protocol transactions.
